Abstract
Bike-sharing programmes have become an important part of the urban transport infrastructure, and there is a need to forecast the short-term demand to prevent bikesharing systems relying without any bicycles or spaces in stations. The Seoul bikesharing experience is a good case to study taking its size and richness of hourly data containing an entire year period. In this paper, we aim to assess various deep learning models in which include individual and ensemble methods for hour bike rental demand forecasting in Seoul. The study constructs a complete feature engineering pipeline and trains various models using historical bike rental, weather, and time-related information from the UCI Seoul Bike Sharing dataset. The study of the best pair architecture for feature combination for temporal demand prediction is also presented and analyzed based on accuracy measure. The results also shed light on what makes a difference for the bike-sharing demand.

Chapter 1. Introduction

Bike-sharing system is one of prominent elements in the urban transportation which provides sustainable and convenient solution for private vehicles and conventional public transit. Bike-sharing programmes have expanded rapidly across cities worldwide, serving millions of users and broadening the range of sustainable transport options with a wider alternative to cars, saving greenhouse-gas emissions and promoting healthier lifestyles. But to optimize these systems it is important to know when and where people will demand bikes.

In past few years, cities such as Seoul have invested on developing their public bike-sharing infrastructure. Seoul's system is spread out over hundreds of stations, carrying millions and a wide range of people who use it daily to get to work or school or for recreation. Demand for bikes fluctuates considerably over the course of each day, as a function of factors such as weather and time of year, day of week, holiday season or time of day. In the morning and evening rush-hour, demand rises steeply as people go back and forth to work, but on weekends or holidays the usage pattern looks quite different. Ridership also declines during adverse weather conditions if there is rain or snow, and some parts of town can just seem too hot or too cold to get on a bike. This fact makes demand prediction a difficult problem, the system needs to consider several features that interact among themselves and that evolve over time.

Effective bike-sharing system relies on precise short-term demand prediction. Station hosts must receive a projection of how many bikes they will need at each spot in the hours ahead, for purposes of rebalancing, to prevent stations from running full or empty, and to maintain seamless service for riders. Inaccurate prediction results in wasted resources and reduced user satisfaction. With the rapid expansion of bike-sharing systems, there is an increasing requirement on effective and accurate data-driven demand forecasting.
The objective of this thesis is to study the performance of deep learning models in predicting hourly bike rental demand in Seoul bike-sharing system. Through historical bike-rental, weather and other relevant features from the UCI Seoul Bike Sharing dataset we study accuracy of different model architectures and how best to predict demand. The analysis provides a detailed understanding of the most important characteristics

1.1 Motivation
In the past 10 years, urban mobility has changed dramatically across the planet. Cities are increasingly turning to sustainable transportation alternatives in response to concerns on traffic congestion, air pollution and carbon emissions. Bike-sharing has become one of the most widespread and fast-expanding shared mobility services. According to Todd, O'Brien & Cheshire; the total number of bike-sharing systems worldwide expanded from 347 in 2010 to over 2000 at the end of 2020, marking an almost six-fold increase within a decade [1]. Worldwide bike-sharing market was estimated at $1.6 billon in 2020, and it is expected to expand at a compounding annual growth rate of 7.9% until to reach by the year 2028 [2].
One of the leading cities in this movement is Seoul, South Korea. The city started the "Seoul Bike" or "Ddareungi," public bike-sharing program in October 2015 with about 150 stations and 1,500 bikes along the Han River [3]. The number of rental stations, allday availability (via mobile apps), and the overall amount of bikes (more than 20,000) increased rapidly [3] Data posted by the Seoul Metropolitan Government indicate that it has 54% peak load (morning and evening rush hours) and 59.6% of the total trips are less than 4 km [4]. The scale and variety of use make Seoul system an interesting case for research in demand forecasting.
The interest in bikes significantly changes over the whole day and is affected with several parameters, such as weather, time of the day, day of week, holidays and seasons. In the morning and evening, peak demand rises as commuters flow to and from their jobs, while weekend days and holidays exhibit different usage completely. Inclement weather, like rain or snow or particularly cold temperatures can also suddenly affect ridership. All these dimensions interact in complex, non-linear ways that are hard to model by general rules or traditional statistical methods.
The implications of bad demand forecasting are real. Empty stations can affect riders from obtaining a bicycle; overflowing stations can prevent riders from returning bikes at their destination. Both scenarios result in an unsatisfied customer, decreased system usage and more operational cost to rebalance the fleet. With the extension of

1.3 Research Questions
RQ 1: Is it possible to use a deep learning model to better describe the intricate nonlinear temporal patterns of Seoul bike-sharing demand, offering accurate hourly predictions?
We will train the single LSTM and TCN models on Seoul Bike dataset with temporal partition. It will be their performances compared to R˛, RMSE and MAE that to test if the deep learning models could become a reliable baseline of short-term bike demand prediction. RQ 2: Is it useful to integrate attention mechanisms in hybrid architectures of forecasting?
This experiment will compare attention-based models (TCN-GRU with Multi-Head Attention and TCN-CBAM-LSTM with Channel and Spatial Attention) against the simpler hybrid architectures without attention. It is intended to check if the increased complexity of attention-based methods results in quantifiable improvement for this forecasting problem. RQ 3: Can hybrid architectures based on a mixture of neural network paradigms improve the performance of each paradigm by itself in short-term bike demand prediction?
A range of hybrid models like TCN-LSTM, TCN-GRU-Attention, TCN-CBAMLSTM and LSTM-XGBoost Multi-Scale-TCN-LSTM will be compared with respective baseline LSTMs and TCNs under same evaluation conditions. The obtained performance measurements will show what benefits can be achieved by the combination of complementary model paradigms.

Chapter 2. State of the Art

This chapter will cover the theoretical underpinnings of the various concepts that make up this thesis work, including time series forecasting, the deep learning architectures employed, the factors influencing bike-sharing demand, followed by a review of the relevant literature and current state of the art.
2.1 Time Series Forecasting
It is the growing collection of large historical data in many scientific and practical areas that has triggered the demand for adequate and efficient forecasting methodologies to extract the stochastic dependence between past patterns and future values, when provided with past observations [8]. Over the past few decades, time series modeling and forecasting have become more and more popular in topics including transportation, energy, medical science, climate science and urban studies with many successful applications in each of above mentioned domains [8][9][10]. When it comes to different sectors, a proper method should be chosen and applied into the raw time series data in order to get more accurate predictions. The basic aim of time series modelling is to analyse historical data, identify any significant pattern(s) and deduce a model that can be subsequently used for predicting future points by extracting some useful information from the past observations.
Time series can be described as a sequence of clearly ordered data points collected at regular time intervals [8]. It is a staple technique in time series analysis, not only allowing to investigate the temporal aspects of some dataset but also to venture a forecasted future based on this temporal behavior. For example, in the field of city-wide transportation, hourly demand data for bike-sharing exhibits strong daily and weekly seasonality due to commuter cycles as well as longer term behavior associated with weather pattern changes [7].
However, time series data often trend or are seasonal or cyclical and that means the relationship between input variables to the model and predicted output changes with time. Observations are not independent since they follow each other, due to the successive nature of the data. So, selecting time-lag discourse value is so important to find data subsets that have a high correlation in among the dependent and independent variables. The generated predictions are then more accurate and precise [10]. Moreover,

17

external influencing factors such as weather effects and holidays or special events may add additional sudden change in the underlying patterns; and thus make the prediction task much more difficult.
For the planning and decision making tasks that would be performed in any applied context, accurate prediction is crucial. To tackle these challenges, various forecasting methods have been proposed including traditional linear statistical methods such as Autoregressive Integrated Moving Average models (ARIMA) [11], gradientboosting-based approaches such as eXtreme Gradient Boosting (XGBoost) [12], and deep learning architectures such as Recurrent Neural Networks (RNN), Long Short-Term Memory (LSTM) networks [13], Temporal Convolutional Networks (TCN) [14] and attention-based models [15]. There have also been very recent work on using transformer architectures for time series forecasting [9] with notable success. But there is no preferred method which would be appropriate for every time series and prediction problem, and the selection of an appropriate approach still depends much on the nature of data, horizon to forecast, and application context.
2.1.1 Supervised Learning and Deep Learning
Machine learning (ML) assumes and form a subset of the field of artificial intelligence that enables algorithms to learn from data and experience and find patterns so that, given an independent input, predictions can be made without extensive human intervention [18]. Unlike conventional algorithms, which adhere to a set of explicitly programmed rules for making decisions, intelligent machine learning algorithms learn directly from data using an iterative methodology and they discover information that is valuable from amongst voluminous amounts [19]. With more data and more diversity of applicable scenarios available, machine learning systems can be trained much better to get the right answer.
Machine learning algorithms can usually be categorized into four main categories supervised, unsupervised, semi-supervised and reinforcement learning [18]. In this thesis, we focus on supervised learning as all models are trained on labelled historical data, i.e. the input features and associated target values are provided.

Supervised machine learning seeks a mapping function from an input feature space X to the corresponding output labels Y, given a set of labeled samples. It tries to find the function Y = f(X) (where X is input, and Y is output), by adjusting the trainable parameters of a given model in such a way that the loss on training data is minimized. After model parameters are well-tuned, the model trained is employed to predict new data which has not been seen before [18].
Deep learning is a subfield of machine learning which is based on artificial neural networks with multiple layers, where higher levels in the hierarchy represent more abstract features, and is used to model complex patterns through its deep structure [19]. Unlike conventional machine learning methods which often involve the manual feature engineering process, deep learning models can automatically learn the informative and non-linear relations from data by means of multiple stacked layers. Each layer in a deep neural network learns higher level representations depending on the weights of its previous layers, which allows the model to pick out patterns that we may not be able to describe or define ourselves.
In the realm of time series prediction, approaches based on deep learning have shown extraordinary performance compared to standard technologies from the fields of statistics and machine learning when handling big data that encapsulate complex chronological relations, non-linear interdependencies and combined input variables [9]. Structures such as Long Short-Term Memory (LSTM) networks [13], Temporal Convolutional Networks (TCN) [14] and Gated Recurrent Units (GRU) [16] have been specially crafted or adapted for sequential data and have outperformed other techniques on a diverse set of forecasting problems [5]. 2.1.2 Recurrent Neural Network (RNN)
An RNN is a type of artificial neural network, these networks are designed to process sequential data with internal hidden states capturing information from previous time steps [20]. While regular feedforward neural networks have connections that flow one way from input to output, RNNs also bring signals back to the network (that is why they are called recurrent). This makes them especially relevant in circumstances in which we care about the order in the data, as it is often the case for time series forecasting, natural language processing, and speech recognition.
An RNN architecture is simply a chain of repeating neural network modules. The network gets the current input and previous hidden state at each time step, computes them together, outputs result along with updating current hidden state in next time step [20]. This allows the network to in theory capture long-range temporal dependencies over the full length of the input sequence.
However, in practice, many RNNs exhibit a well-documented problem called the vanishing gradient problem [21]. Gradients are passed backward in time and used to update the weights during training. As the length of sequences grows those gradients are repeatedly multiplied by some small value and they shrink rapidly (exponentially) to zero. This makes learning of long-term dependencies in the network very hard, as the effective trace of earlier time-steps fades away quickly after learning progresses. On the other hand, in some cases, gradients may explode that is called exploding gradient problem [21].

2.1.3 Long Short-Term Memory (LSTM)
The Long Short-Term Memory (LSTM) was proposed by Hochreiter and Schmidhuber [13] in 1997, motivated by the vanishing gradient problem affecting standard RNNs. The long, short term memory (LSTM) model introduces a dedicated memory cell and gating scheme, which enables the network to retain, modify or forget information for distant time-steps. This structure enables the LSTMs to model short-term and long-term temporal dependencies, and as such are one of the most popular architectures for time series forecasting problems [9].

Figure 4: LSTM Network [24] 21

At a basic level, the LSTM architecture is comprised of four interacting "entities", namely Memory Cells, Input Gate, Forget Gate and Output Gate. The forget gate decides which information about the previous cell state should be thrown away upon the combination with the current input and last hidden state. The input gate decides what new information should be stored to the cell state. The cell state itself can serve as a conveyer belt to carry relevant information across time steps and thus counteract the loss of information with little degradation. The output gate determines which part of the cell state will be exposed as a hidden state output at the current time step [13]. Now the network can learn which pieces of information are important to remember and which can be forgotten, mitigating the long-term dependency issue that traditional RNNs have problems. Related work in bike-sharing demand prediction, Min and Jung [25] compared three models with a dataset consisting of daily data for 389 Seoul bike stations. The LSTM model was shown to capture the intricate temporal dynamics in bike usage data and yielded better predictions than a variety of baseline approaches, especially under high demand variation (e.g., rush hours and weather-related events). Together, these studies are a testament to the powerful capacity of LSTM networks relating to capturing temporal dependencies for transportation prediction tasks and indicate that using LSTM in combination with other deep or handengineered mechanisms could lead to greater predictive performance.
2.1.4 Gated Recurrent Units (GRU)
Gated Recurrent Unit (GRU) was proposed by Cho et al. [16] as an easier, more elegant solution to the LSTM architecture. Whereas LSTMs have three individual gates and their own cell state to facilitate the control of information flow, the GRU uses two gates instead and does not hold a separate cell state. The two gates in a GRU are called the update gate and the reset gate. Consequently, the model has fewer trained parameters compared to an LSTM, which makes for faster training and lower computational costs while still being able to capture long-range dependencies on sequential data [16].
In a GRU, the update gate determines how much of the previous hidden state should be propagated to current time step, which essentially unifies functions of the forget and input gates in an LSTM. The closer the update gate towards one, the more

SRH

23

previous information to be maintained by the network and when it approaches zero, then the newly computed candidate information is retained. The reset gate controls the degree to which we should forget the previous hidden state. When the value of reset gate is close to zero, the model in such configuration forgets past context and acts as if it were reading the first element in sequence which can be beneficial for modeling short-term dependencies where further information from distant past may not have any significant effect [18].

In the field of traffic flow forecasting, Fu et al. [28] compared prediction models to predict traffic speed and volume on urban road networks using LSTM and GRU. The conclusion is that the two architectures yielded similar prediction performances, with the GRU model achieving faster training due to its simple internal structure and smaller number of parameters. 2.1.5 Temporal Convolutional Networks (TCN)
Temporal Convolutional Networks (TCN) were proposed in Bai et al. [14] in 2018 as an alternative to state-of-the-art recurrent networks, traditionally believed to be more naturally suited for temporal based data processing. The TCN is based on three

SRH

25

fundamental principles causal convolutions, dilated convolutions, and residual connections. These parts form a whole and allow the network to process sequential data in an efficient manner and at the same time they keep the power of capturing dependencies for wide range of time scales.
Causal convolutions ensure that the output at any time step depends only on current and past inputs, thereby preventing any information leakage from the future. This property is crucial for time series forecast, as it would cause data leakage and too optimistic results if we use future data during prediction [14]. However, a drawback of traditional causality-based convolutions is that the attention over past time steps of processed data is limited by the kernel size and depth limit of the network. Dealing with long-range temporal dependencies within this constraint would either demand very deep networks or large filter sizes, both of which entail computational and optimization difficulties.
Dilated convolutions were created to overcome this issue. Dilated convolutions increase the size of network's receptive field without increasing complexity or computational load by introducing gaps between filter elements. If the expansion factor is increased exponentially (e.g., with rates of 1, 2, 4, 8 and 16) across the layers then we can get a very large receptive field size even for a small number of layers [14]. This enables the TCN to both capture fine-grained short-term patterns, e.g., hourly swings in demand for bikes, and longer-term dependencies (weekly or seasonal cycles).

This contributes to alleviating the degradation issue so that increasing the network depth in a deep architecture does not simply lead to an increased error of training but well performed [29]. The success of TCN on capturing multi-scale temporal patterns owing to the dilated convolutional structure, makes it ideal for bikesharing demand forecasting since demand is affected by hourly commuting pattern, daily weather variations and weekly usage cycle. In this work, TCN operates as both an independent baseline model and a feature extraction backbone component of various hybrid networks.
2.1.6 Attention Mechanisms
The attention mechanism was first proposed in the field of neural machine translation by Bahdanau et al. [34], to enabling the decoder in a sequence-to-sequence model to attend selectively to different segments of the input sequence when generating each output element. Instead of reducing the whole input to a fixed-length vector, the attention mechanism computes weighted sums over all input positions where the weights were computed based on which position is most important in deciding the current prediction. This idea was later generalized and expanded by Vaswani et al. [15] in the self-attention and Transformer architecture, that have since been developed to be a ubiquitous building block across many domains such as natural language processing (NLP), computer vision, and time series Forecasting.
For self-attention, we compare every time step in the input against all other time steps to calculate a set of attention weights. These weights indicate the relevance or dependence of elements on each other, enabling the model to learn long-range dependencies throughout the sequence Luong et al [35]. The multi-head attention version addresses this by performing several attention operations in parallel, each with its learned set of projection parameters [15]. The outputs of these learned attention heads are then concatenated and linearly transformed to get the final results. By utilizing multiple heads, the model can attend to information from different representation subspaces at

SRH

28

different positions concurrently which enhances its ability to learn diverse temporal patterns in the data.
Figure 10: CBAM architecture with channel and spatial attention modules [17]
Another type of attentions mechanism is the Convolutional Block Attention Module (CBAM) due to Woo et al. [17]. Compared with self-attention which performs attention over position in sequence, CBAM conducts attention at two different aspects channel attention and spatial attention. The channel attention module learns which feature channels are more informative by pooling spatial clues, while the spatial attention module learns to allocate the limited transmitted source information from some positions of input image. These two modules are utilized in a serial manner, to recalculate its feature channel-wisely modulation and then the spatial or temporal position refinement [17]. The large-scale study of Weiwei Jiang [5] shows that attention-augmented models outperform classic recurrent baselines across bike-sharing and transportation demand-forecasting in general, but they particularly shine when expected variances are high (i.e., during peak hours and weather-related demand transitions). The attention weights also offer some level of interpretability, to the extend that we can see which historical time steps the model finds more useful as input features while making each prediction.
Still, attention mechanisms are not always superior. Their effectiveness is greatly influenced by data nature, targeted temporal pattern complexity. Attention also adds an extra layer to the architecture, increasing the number of trainable parameters and computational cost, which can cause over-fitting on smaller datasets if not correctly regularised. In this thesis, both Multi-Head Attention as well as CBAM are used in hybrid architectures to investigate whether the extra complexity from attention is superior for hourly bike demand forecasting.
2.1.7 XGBoost
The ensemble method is built on the idea of training a large number of models from which complimentary subsets might be combined to make improved, more robust predictions than any individual model. Early work by Breiman has already shown that averaging the predictions of many predictors learned on boostrap samples of the data, or bagging, yields a significant reduction of variance and improvements in generalization [36]. Soon, thereafter however, AdaBoost was introduced by Freund and Schapire an algorithm that trains weak learners in sequence each focusing on the examples that previous learner has mistake on to transform a group of moderately accurate models into one superiorly accurate model [37]. Leveraging these works, Friedman introduced a more general framework called gradient boosting to solve the problem of ensemble learning as a numerical optimization over function space [38]. Instead of reweighting misclassified examples, for the next model trained by gradient boosting, the fitting we are doing is to negative gradients of a loss function chosen at previous steps, making it easily adaptable to any differentiable objective. If decision trees are used as the base classifiers, the

method is found to perform very well in a wide range of application domains while the original definition was not scalable for data with large number of instances and attributes.

XGBoost, or eXtreme gradient boosting for short, (Chen and Guestrin) is a powerful implementation of gradient Boosted Decision Trees that was released by the same name as open-source software [12]. The system was widely adopted, in both academia and industry with its implementation being the prevalent algorithm at structured data notebook competions while also powering production systems of leading technology companies. A variety of technical advances differentiates XGBoost from previous incarnations. The first is to expose explicit regularization in the objective function, by penalizing the number of leaves and weight size that principle provides a general way to computationally adjust model complexity for machine learning scope which was less available in previous tree boosting methods [12]. The second is a weighted quantile sketch algorithm, which makes it efficient to identify approximate splits through the construction of split candidates that are made according to the distribution of secondorder gradient statistics and hence allows scaling for datasets with hundreds-of-millions examples [12]. Third, we provide a sparsity-aware algorithm which trains the default direction of missing values at each split to handle sparse data efficiently without preprocessing inspection [12]. 2.2 Feature Engineering for Time Series
Forecasting performance is a joint function of the architecture and quality of the input features to the model. For bike-sharing demand quantity prediction, using the raw hourly counts and meteorological measurements directly cannot fully reflect the complicated patterns beneath because multiple factors are actually affecting ridership through interaction and they distribute on a different range of time scale. Feature engineering deals with this via the transformation of raw data into a more informative set of input variables, which make these patterns more readily apparent to the learning algorithm [7].
The simplest is generating lag feature, which will allow the model to access previous values of target variable. There are strong temporal dependencies in the hourly demand, where 1 hour lag could capture short-term momentum, a 24-hour lag would contain daily commuting cycle and a 168-hour lag represents weekly periodicity [9]. By incorporating lag horizons at multiple time scales, the model can have simultaneous access to both the immediate past, as well as what happened n hours ago on the same day and one week ago at n-th hour of a day which is essential to capture both regular commuting patterns (due to people's daily routine) and event driven variation. Rolling window statistics act as a complement to lag features by taking aggregate measures like the average, standard deviation over a sliding window of recent observations, smoothing short-term noise and capturing local trends in demand [10].
Temporal features like hour of the day and day of week contain valuable information on what to expect in demand levels but treating them as plain integers is not

SRH

33

representative of their cyclical nature. Hour 23 is "next to" hour 0, in the sense of being adjacent in time but maximally distant as integers, and likewise for Sunday and Monday. Cyclical encoding addresses this by representing each temporal variable using sine and cosine components which maintain circular continuity while allowing the model to learn smooth periodic patterns that spans cycle boundaries [10].

Figure 14: Lag features and cyclical time encoding for hourly time-series modelling [47]

The temporal behaviour of weather variables often interacts in a non-linear way, which is not reflected by raw features. Temperature has an inverted-U relationship with ridership, for example, and a warm morning rush hour creates far different demand than a rainy morning during which it is equally warm. Using the same Seoul Bike Sharing dataset used in this thesis, Sathishkumar and Cho (2020) explicitly found through their variable importance analysis that temperature and hour of the day are the top two important predictors for hourly demand [7]. They can add dummy variables to represent these so that the model has an easy way to incorporate this conditional information. Binary features for holiday, weekend and system operational status further contribute to context not inferred from the continuous features. Combined, these methods make up the feature engineering pipeline used in this thesis where the raw data is transformed into a high-dimensional input space for training and testing the demand forecasting model.

2.3 Bike-Sharing Systems and Demand Forecasting
Bike-sharing programs have developed over the decades, through a series of distinct generations, beginning with the experimental deployment of free bikes in Amsterdam in 1965 (a program that quickly folded because the majority of its bikes were stolen or vandalized). In the 1990s coin-deposited systems was first introduced in Copenhagen and early 2000, smart card access, GPS tracking and automatic stations were introduced as third generation docked system [1]. The current generation is appbased, the apps serve to find, unlock, and provide payment for the bikes scaling city per city. As of the end of 2020, more than 2,000 bicycle-sharing systems were deployed worldwide, which has a scale of about 1.6 billion USD and is still growing through the current decade, as per by [1]. These have been increasingly incorporated into urban mobility plans, providing a low emission last mile solution in combination with highcapacity public transport.

SRH

Figure 15: Seoul map with bike stations [48] 35

Public bike-sharing system in Seoul, Ddareungi was introduced along the Han River in October 2015 with approximately 150 stations and 1,500 bikes [3]. The system had grown rapidly, with more than 20,000 bikes at over 1,500 rental stations available in an instant through mobile apps [3]. According to the Seoul Metropolitan Government Usage data, 54 percent of daily trips are rush-hours (in the mornings and evenings) and that almost 60 percent of such trips go less than four kilometers [4]. This scale, wide variety of usage patterns and rich hourly information makes the Seoul system an interesting case for demand forecasting research.
The market demand for shared bicycles is determined by multiple types of factors. Weather has a considerable impact, with mild temperatures having a positive effect on cycling, and rain, snow, cold and heat having the opposite effect. Sathishkumar, Cho (2020) found that the temperature and hour of day are among the first two most important input factors contributing to hourly bike rentals in Seoul scheme [7]. Temporal dimension the demand during weekdays is characterized by bimodal commuting peaks in the morning and evening, while holidays or weekends generate flatter profiles compared to weekdays, because passengers normally use public transport for leisure purposes. These effects are magnified over seasonal variations where winter months see far less ridership than summer. Those factors don't operate in isolation, but bounce off each other and combine together in complex, non-linear ways a warm day with the sun overhead on a Thursday morning during rush hour will produce very different demand levels than that exact same warm weather combined with rain clouds on a holiday afternoon which makes it difficult to forecast accurately for any individual modeling effort.
The central operational value of demand prediction lies in repositioning, a challenge common to all station-based bike-sharing systems. Since users take and return bikes at different places, stations gradually drift to empty or full, which both compromise user experience and reduce system usage. To maintain operation, operators must redistribute bicycles in the network, and this is achieved efficiently based on good and reliable short-term predictions where demand will come up [1]. Miscalculated predictions result in empty stations that irritate riders and overflowing stations where returns can't be made, increased cost from reactive operations vs proactive rebalancing. With today's systems growing rapidly, the separation between responsive management and prediction-fuelled operations is growing larger, making accurate demand forecasting more critical to a sustainable system.

2.4 Related Work

In the following sections, we will discuss relevant related works for demand forecasting in bike-sharing and other transportation systems. The review is organized based on the methodological paradigm, covering from conventional statistical methods to machine learning and deep learning approaches and towards the recent hybrid architectures and attention-based models. For each category, exemplary studies are presented with focus on the used data sets, forecasting setting and their results. This framework is representative for the whole research field and is used to position the current work with respect to existing research, and to identify what exact gaps this thesis will try to fill.

2.4.1 Traditional and Statistical Approaches

The first generation of bike-sharing demand prediction studies mostly leveraged classical time series operation models and regression approaches best practices in common transportation planning activities. Autoregressive integrated moving average (ARIMA) models were one of the earliest techniques used, given that they offer a established framework for modeling univariate time series with linear temporal dependencies through autoregressive, differencing and moving average components [11]. Although ARIMA and its seasonal extension SARIMA can provide decent short-term forecasting results when the signal process is rather linear trend-stationary, they fail to capture the non-linear relationships between weather, time and demand as they seem inherent to bike-sharing usage patterns.
Regression models Generalizations of the previous framework were suggested by directly incorporating external variables like temperature, humidity, wind speed and calendar effects in addition to the temporal dimension of the series. Sathishkumar and Cho (2020) utilized numerous rule-based and regression models on the Seoul Bike Sharing dataset that is used in this thesis, their most successful model CUBIST obtained ar R-squared of around 95% under the test data [7]. This result set a very high bar for the dataset and also showed that judiciously designed statistical models including weather as an attribute can account for most of the variability in hourly demand. However, the evaluation protocol in that work employed same-time prediction, in which the model was allowed to use current hour's weather observations when predicting current hour demand. In an operational scenario, predictions need to be made based on conditions yet to be

SRH

37

observed, which makes this a stronger and harder prediction task. Min and Jung (2021) compared vector autoregression with machine learning and deep learning baselines on daily bike-sharing data from 389 stations in Seoul; while the VAR model could capture overall temporal trends, it was outperformed across their comparison by non-linear methods [25].
These studies demonstrate the clear picture of conventional methods. Statistical models have the advantages of interpretability, strong theoretical properties and simple computational need, which are used as baseline model for demand forecasting. Meanwhile, the linearity assumption and their limited ability to capture complex interaction effects of covariates impede their performance in situations where demand is influenced by weather condition, day-of-week/season cycles as well as contextual features collectively. Because of such limitation, the focus has moved towards machine learning and in later days deep learning approaches that are described in the subsequent sections.
2.4.2 Machine Learning Approaches
Pitfalls of linear statistical models capturing complex interactions among weather, temporal and ridership fed the research to implement machine learning techniques for forecasting bike-demand. Compared to regression-based methods, which require the practitioner to specify the functional form of input-output relationship, machine learning algorithms like random forest and gradient boosting are inherently capable of learning non-linear mappings from data and are well suited to the multi-factorial aspect of demand prediction.
Aghe evaluated a variety of standard machine learning regressors on the Seoul Bike Sharing dataset, which included linear regression, decision tree regression, random forest, bagging regressor and stacking regressor from combining multiple base learners [40]. The one with the best performance on test set was stack ensemble (R-squared : 89.56 percent), which means meta-learning to combine models in different ways can increase generalization than using just a single algorithm. Ergöl Aydin et al. Also did a specific comparison of gradient boosting algorithms for bike-sharing demand They compared XGBoost, LightGBM and CatBoost on real-world datasets from Konya and Washington D.C. [39]. All the three algorithms reached strong prediction accuracy and CatBoost demonstrated slightly better generalization performance after hyperparameter tuning. Common predictors between both studies included temperature (°F) and time of

SRH

38

day and confirmed the importance of weather-temporal effects as a primary driver behind energy demand.
These machine learning techniques have practical advantages. Ensembles of decision trees are used in practice on account of their ability to naturally accommodate mixed types of features, and they have become popular because they minimize the amount of preprocessing needed and provide automatic importance scores that give insight into which variable is inducing predictions. These methods process each individual data point independently and do not capture the notion of temporal dependency between subsequent time steps. A prediction for a time point such as 8 AM on a day is issued in isolation and without structural knowledge of what actually happened at 7 AM or what usually occurs at 8 AM on the same type of days, unless these orderings are explicitly included via feature engineering. Due to their dependence on hand-engineered temporal features, the amount of sequential structure that these models can exploit is a priori bounded and it is precisely this bound that deep learning methods presented in the next section try to overcome, by allowing the model itself to learn suitable temporal representations directly from raw sequence.
2.4.3 Deep Learning Approaches
Deep learning approaches overcame the limitation of sequential modeling in machine learning by automatically extracting temporal representations from raw sequences without the need for engineered lag and window properties. Min and Jung (2021) also evaluated a vector autoregression model, support vector regression model, and LSTM for public bicycle demand in Seoul with the daily data of 389 stations. Their results showed that the best performing model was an LSTM that has high complexity than other models introduced by them, supporting our argument of recurrent architecture being more apt at catching temporal domain dependencies in ridership data [25]. LSTM networks, for the prediction of station-free bike-sharing demand in Nanjing and obtained at least acceptable accuracy in periods from ten to thirty minutes indicating that deep learning systems were capable of processing more fine-grained temporal patterns necessary for operational planning.
The implementation of (de)convolutional architectures has also been investigated. Lin et al. (2018) proposed a graph convolutional neural network for station level hourly demand prediction in a large-scale bike-sharing network, taking spatial relationships

SRH

39

between stations into consideration by capturing the geographic dependence ignored by purely temporal models [33]. Li et al. presented an irregular convolutional neural network coupled with LSTM for short-term bike-sharing demand forecasting, where geographically distant stations can have similar usage patterns because of the similarities in their functionality [41]. Their IrConv-LSTM model was tested in 5 cities (Singapore, Chicago, Washington D.C., New York and London) and performed better than standard benchmarks for any of them. A recent extensive review on deep learning for bike-sharing prediction by Jiang noted that the field has witnessed much advancements with especially the developments of recurrent and convolutional architectures, however due to most works only evaluate one or two models in isolation it was difficult to compare techniques [5].
These deep learning models have shown clear superiority over traditional and machine-learning baselines in capturing temporal and spatial patterns. Yet, each architecture has its own limitations RNNs are good at capturing sequential dependencies at the expense of long training time and difficulty dealing with very long-range patterns; CNNs advocate computational efficiency and parallelism but does not have an explicit memory mechanism. These strengths in complementary guided hybrid systems have driven the design of hybrid structures integrating multiple neural network components, which will be discussed in the next section.
2.4.4 Hybrid and Attention-Based Approaches
The observation that different architectural features pick up complementary aspects of time series data has prompted a recent surge in research on hybrid models, which combine different neural network families inside one forecasting framework. Zhou et al. developed a hybrid TCN-GRU model for short-term bike-sharing demand prediction and tested it with London data from 2015 to 2017 (R-squared 0.984 with MAE and RMSE that is at least 1.98% lower respectively than standalone LSTM, GRU and ARIMA models as baselines) [42]. They used the TCN component to capture local temporal pattern by dilated causal convolutions and a GRU to model dependencies between extracted features, their results showed that their combined approach improved both components taken separately.
Beyond these hybrid architectures even more powerful models have been developed based on the idea that it should be possible to increase the importance of truly

In the area of bike sharing and its relation to other transportation aspects, we have seen an increasing number of paper that demonstrate better-performing models by incorporating attention into the framework of LSTM [5]. The attention weights also have a certain interpretability, showing which historical time steps, the model deems most informative when making a particular prediction, thus providing operational insight into the temporal demand drivers. Newer work has also looked into channel and spatial attention modules that act not only in the time dimension across timesteps but also across feature dimensions, letting the model to focus dynamically on which input variables are important for each prediction step.

2.6 Summary and Discussions
This chapter has presented the theoretical background and state-of-the-art literature in data-driven bike-sharing demand forecasting, including recurrent neural networks, attentions, feedforward deep neural networks with static/dynamic structure data input, gradient boosting decision-trees based, feature engineering-based methods as well as real-world operation scenario of bike-sharing systems. The related work also discussed how these methods have been used in statistics based, machine learning based ensembles and mixed deep learning models for the entire field.

SRH

43

The following review highlights three main voids that are addressed in this work. The first concerns evaluation methodology. The best-known result on the Seoul Bike Sharing dataset (0.95 R-squared), by Sathishkumar and Cho (2020) [7], was achieved under a same-time prediction setting, i.e., the model has access to current hour weather when predicting for the current demand. That's not a real forecast scenario at all. We employ rigorous one-step-ahead forecast by forecasting the bike demand at hour t- 1 using the data until its previous time step (hour t).
The second gap concerns scope of comparison. Current studies commonly only evaluate models of one paradigm machine learning studies compare among GBDT avatars but not deep learning [39], and vice-versa between different deep learning models, where studies test only two or three architectures rather than hybrids or boosting counterparts [25] and study about hybrid combination show just a single configuration that is not compared with other designs [42]. To our best knowledge, no work makes the systematic comparison among single models, hybrid architectures, attention-augmented model and neural-boosting ensemble with exact the same setting.
The third gap consists in under-explored hybrid techniques for bike-sharing such as mixing channel-spatial attention modules with temporal networks, multi-scale convolutional processing and recurrent modelling, two-stage deep learning feature extraction with XGBoost regression model.
We close these gaps with our study that includes 7 architectures: LSTM, TCN, TCN-LSTM, TCN-GRU-Attention, TCN-CBAM-LSTM, LSTM-XGBoost,and Multi-Scale, TCN-LSTM. We choose those to cover the entire range between purely individual models and increasingly complex hybrids. All models are trained on same feature set, they follow the same temporal split pattern and compare under strictly one-step-ahead conditions so that a direct head to-head comparison can be done to show which architectural decisions result in meaningful evidence on how well it plans compared to adding complexity without much return.

3.1 Dataset Description
The data set used in this research is an instance of Seoul Bike Sharing Demand dataset created by Sathishkumar and Cho [7] and available from the UCI Machine Learning Repository. It records the number of bicycles rented per hour from Seoul's public bicycle sharing system for a year: from December 2017 to November 2018. It was important to choose a full calendar year. In this case, the data would naturally show how demand shifted among all four seasons instead of focusing on just the most extreme temperature, both sub zero temperature of Korean winter lows and summer's elevated humidity and Heavy rainfall from monsoon rains coming around July. Without the complete cycle of seasons, a model would lack exposure to seasonal patterns absent from training period.
A noticeable practical advantage of this dataset is its completeness. There are no gaps in the 8,760 hourly records (a quality rarely found in real operational transport data) and no imputation or interpolation is required prior to modelling. The target variable the number of bikes rented in each hour varies substantially, from zero on quiet nights in winter to 3,556 during peak hours. The general average hovers at about 704.6 rentals an hour, but the average is somewhat misleading. Demand follows a heavily right skewed distribution the vast majority of hours are well below average, but a handful of peak hours during weekday morning and evening commutes produce the rental counts far above the mean. A distribution like this is typical with urban transportation systems, and this distributional character is noted here because it also helps to define what "good performance" looks like a model that works well on average can fail substantially when the system is at its most stressed, precisely the conditions under which accurate predictions are most operationally valuable.

Figure 17: Monthly bike rental demand bar chart [7] [author generated]
The raw data was provided with thirteen attributes in addition to the rental count. There are five of these that represent time and context, hour, day of week, month, season (which has categories Spring, Summer, Autumn or Winter) and whether the date is either a public holiday or some sort of non-working day in Korea. The other eight record weather every hour temperature in Celsius, relative humidity, wind speed, visibility, solar radiation, rainfall and snowfall. These variables provide a reasonable characteristic of what the environment was like for each rental observation, but they provide no information about what rental demand looked like in the preceding hours, or even days, before an observed occurrence. The feature engineering procedure proposed in Section 3.2 aims to fill this gap.

3.2 Data Preprocessing and Feature Engineering Plan
Before a model can do anything with this data, it must first be transformed into a representation suitable for sequence-based modelling architectures.
The most important decision that faces one now is how to slice up one year's worth of data into training chunks and testing chunks Random splitting would be a serious mistake for this kind of problem. It works perfectly well in problems such as image classification, where each data point is independent. But if a random sample of hours ends up in the test set, then almost certainly the hours immediately before and after them will be used to train and not test model will already have seen answer. Results obtained under such a setup are therefore likely to be inflate. So, the split we use here is sequential: the first eighty percent of the time series goes to training; then 20% is left out as testing data. There is no overlap. When one evaluates the model, it's always evaluated about hours which have genuinely never been fed in, and this matches how an actual deployed system would operate.

SRH

47

Figure 19: Diagram of the chronological 80/20 train-test split along a timeline. [author generated]
One practical issue is that raw features of different scales cost more to create and maintain. In our old units, temperature might range from -10 to 35 degrees Celsius, while solar radiation is measured in fractions of megajoules, and visibility runs anywhere up to five kilometres or even more. If these are left intact, a learning algorithm tends to emphasize the variable with the larger absolute numbers, regardless of whether it is best predictor. Standardisation fixes this by shifting each feature so that its training-set values centre around zero normally one off from there. One detail matter here, the mean and standard deviation used for this transformation come only from training data. The test set is scaled using those same training-derived numbers, not any statistics it can generate itself. This prevents the subtle information leak when future information leaks into the training process through seemingly innocuous channels such as scaling parameters into model training. The rental count target is treated a similar way but with a scaler of its own, so predictions can be converted back into actual bike counts once the model has made them.

SRH

Figure 20: Illustration of the sliding window. [author generated] 48

Once all the data has been scaled, time to start turning it into sequences. This study's models did not look at one hour in isolation but searched for patterns over consecutive hours. The window size here 24 hours, the model sees the entire context of one day and its job is to forecast as accurately as possible how many bikes will be rented in the coming hour. This window then slides one hour further along, and so the cycle renews itself It creates thousands upon thousands of training examples out of what's just one long year's worth of measurements. Twenty-four hours was a reasonably straightforward choice, because it captures a full daily cycle, from the quiet of night to morning's all-out rush hour following by noontime relaxation and finally evening's peak all within a single data window.
The features' engineering plan is where they build real predictive power. The weather and calendar are brought in fourteen raw variables, but these say nothing about what has been happening to demand recently at all. That's a huge gap; bike sharing is largely self-referential. For instance, this year's busy hour will also tend to be next year's busy hour; today's pattern at this time of day can be surprisingly good information about tomorrow. Two engineering phases are anticipated. The first phase deliberately expands the feature set by creating new variables: lag features to record what happened one, twenty-four, and one hundred sixty-eight hours ago; rolling averages and variability measures over windows that are recent; cyclical encodings to represent hours and days as smooth periodic signals rather than arbitrary integers; rush-hour flags; and weather interaction terms that give a sense of how the combination of temperature and time of day affects ridership. This expansion will bring the number of features up from 14 to 64.

SRH

Figure 21: Feature engineering pipeline [author generated] 49

The second phase then winnows that list. Not all 64 features carry independent information some of them are near duplicates, while others can be practically perfectly reconstructed out of a few of their neighbours. An analysis of correlations hears out which of these redundant variables is now redundant with each other and a multicollinearity check will identify features that add computational cost but no predictive advantage. The target is a final set of around 30 features that keeps everything useful from 64 while excising the noise and duplication.
But there is one non-negotiable, the model must not know any information from the hour it would predict. Not the rentals, nor anything taken from that timeslot in real time. If this rule gets broken, perhaps only in an innocent momentary drift, the model takes a shortcut to solving problem rather than learning how reality behaves. Then all reported accuracies at once no longer mean anything outside a lab setting
3.3 Modelling Approach
The literature review in Chapter 2 established that individual deep learning architectures, while capable of modelling temporal patterns in bike-sharing demand, face inherent limitations in predictive accuracy when applied in isolation. At the same time, the gap analysis in Section 2.6 revealed that hourly demand forecasting under strict temporal evaluation conditions remains underexplored, particularly with hybrid architectures that combine complementary modelling paradigms. This thesis addresses that gap by systematically constructing and evaluating seven distinct architectures, all operating under identical experimental conditions. Each model receives the same thirty optimised features arranged in twenty-four-hour input sequences and produces a single output: the predicted rental count for the next hour. Because every other variable is held constant, any observed difference in performance can be attributed solely to the architectural design. As introduced in Section 1.4, the overall research process follows the CRISP-DM framework [6], whose iterative stages map directly onto the structure of this thesis: Sections 3.1 and 3.2 correspond to data understanding and data preparation, this section defines the modelling strategy, Chapter 4 addresses implementation, and Chapter 5 covers evaluation.
An important methodological choice relates to the target of prediction. A few previous works on the Seoul dataset particularly [7] and [40], provide calendar and weather data from the GMT hour to which the prediction is to be made, thus giving implicit

SRH

50

access to up-to-date conditions. Under such setting, the task is more like an interpolation rather than a real forecasting. The approaches presented in this thesis, instead, adhere to a strict one-step-ahead scheme, for any extended look-back window, only the timestamped data recorded 24 hours beforehand is provided as input and each model is expected to predict demand at that subsequent hour without seeing any realizations of the future. The resulting level of accuracy is, of course, less than those achieved by realtime techniques, but a system that could actually use.
The seven architectures are organised into two tiers. The first tier comprises two individual baselines. One adopts a recurrent design [13] that processes input sequences step by step, maintaining an internal state to capture temporal dependencies. The other employs a convolutional approach [14] that captures temporal patterns through progressively wider receptive fields rather than through recurrence. Together, these two baselines establish a performance floor that any hybrid must surpass to justify its added complexity.
The second set level includes five hybrids, the models of which were developed in order to validate a given hypothesis identified in the gap analysis presented in Section 2. The first hybrid iteratively chains a convolutional feature extractor to a recurrent sequence processor, exploring whether the input-output relationship between the two paradigms can lead one to learn richer temporal features for the other. The second hybrid uses a gated recurrent unit (GRU) [16] in place of the recurrent element and multiplicative attention mechanism [15], to see whether selectively giving different parts of a sequence more weight can be used to reduce prediction error even further, without sacrificing the interpretability provided by learned attention weights. The third hybrid is the original contribution of this thesis, such model adds a channel and spatial attention module between the convolutional and recurrent blocks, that allows the network to focus on locally important features of the captured temporal representation. This attention mechanism has not been used for hourly bike-sharing demand prediction before. The fourth architecture adopts a multi-stage ensemble approach. At the first stage, a recurrent network generates compact learnable representations for every input window; at the second stage, these representations are then concatenated to the original tabular features and fed into a gradient-boosted tree (GBT) regressor that combines virtual based sequential pattern recognition with GBT based capability in handling heterogeneous tabular data sources. The fifth hybrid involves multi-parallel convolutional branches which

SRH

51

process the data at different kernel width simultaneously to get patterns over various temporal scales. The features produced by these branches are then concatenated and processed by a RNN to capture dependencies in the resulting multi-scale representation.

Figure 22: Seven architectures organised by type and design principle. [author generated]

This highly managed step-towards incremental design makes it possible for the evaluation in Chapter 5 to focus on extracting the contribution of each architectural decision, without being affected by multiple simultaneous changes. Implementation details (i.e., layer setups, training schemes, framework selections) are left to Chapter 4.

3.4 Evaluation Plan

Although the simple concept of assessing a forecast model is the process is where the idea differs what turns out to be of consequence as much as outcome itself. Don't set results by accuracy numbers reported in studies, two studies using the same dataset may report substantially different results simply because their evaluation protocols differ. In this section, the evaluation plan to be used in the article presented here is outlined and its motivation is explained. This work reports only performance numbers that come from the test set the last twenty percent of a time series that no model has seen during training. No random cross-validation is done, because random sampling disrupts the temporal ordering of observations and produces overly optimistic estimates of deployment performance. The test set must always lie strictly after the training set in chronological order. an R˛ of 0.85 indicates that the model captures most of the variance in demand but leaves fifteen percent unexplained. RMSE, or root mean squared error, gives the average prediction error per bike and penalizes larger errors more heavily than will small ones; this matters because a prediction error of five hundred bikes during peak hours is

SRH

52

substantially more costly operationally than making off by fifty during a quiet night. MAE, the mean absolute error, is a fairer measure that regards all mistakes equitably. Putting all three together paints a fuller picture not only, may two models have similar R˛ but different error profiles, also the RMSE-to-MAE ratio tells us whether a model's mistakes are spread out evenly or tend toward extremes. Beyond aggregate numbers, the evaluation also includes comparisons along two axes. In addition to the aggregate model comparison, a component-level analysis is planned for the best-performing architecture, in which individual components are removed to quantify their respective contributions to the overall prediction accuracy. The first is individual versus hybrid do hybrid architectures really produce better results than the base lines, and if they do how much? The second axis is cost versus results if a model has twice as many parameters, then it must make realistically better predictions; if not, then the simpler model is preferable. Overfitting is assessed by comparing each model's training set accuracy against its actual test set performance a large gap between the two is an indicator that the model has memorised training patterns without generalising to unseen data. Wherever possible, time of day and weather conditions are also considered in analyses of prediction errors because a model that performs well on aggregate metrics but systematically underperforms during evening rush hours or rainy mornings may be of limited operational value.

Figure 23: Evaluation framework diagram. [author generated]

Three quantitative metrics are used throughout this study to assess model performance. The coefficient of determination (R˛) measures the proportion of variance

SRH

53

in hourly demand explained by the model, with a value of 1.0 representing a perfect fit. The Root Mean Squared Error (RMSE) expresses prediction error in the original unit of bikes per hour and penalises large deviations more heavily due to the squaring operation. The Mean Absolute Error (MAE) assigns equal weight to all errors and provides an interpretable average deviation. The formal definitions are given in Figure 24.

4.2 Data Preprocessing Implementation
The temporal split described in Section 3.2 results in a training set of 6,840 instances (1 December 2017 - 18 September 2018) and a test set of 1,752 instances (19September - 30November 2018), that have no samples in common. Two scikit-learn Standard Scaler instances were fitted exclusively on the training partition. one for the input features and one for the target variable. By using training-set only statistics for both the train and test sets, information leakage is prevented, and by retaining the fitted target scaler, model predictions can be inverse-transformed to the original bike-count scale.
The time series is then scaled and encoded as overlapping input/output data pairs with a sliding window of length 24. Both tensor inputs have the shape of (24, 30) to represent 24 hours for each over 30 features with the label is target i-24 at one hour pasted current window. In this configuration, only the one-step-ahead rule is rigidly enforced as no information from the prediction horizon was back-fed into the input. This approach leads to 6,816 training and 1,728 test sequences where the last 20% of each training sequence is employed as a chronological validation subset for early stopping as well as learning rate decay. During creation, six types of feature categories were established. The one-hour delay had the strongest single correlation of r = 0.908 with target. 4.4.3 TCN-LSTM
The TCN-LSTM is the first of two hybrid architectures introduced in this thesis and combines two established "Temporal Convolutional" and "Recurrent" paradigms, as introduced in Sections 2.1.5, 2.1.3 within a shared sequence-to-sequence architecture. Based on the complementary advantages we observed in the literature, TCN can capture multi-scale local temporal patterns with its dilated convolutions, while LSTM encodes long-range dependencies among such features with its gating mechanism. Instead of treating the raw input symmetrically, the two parts are stacked in a sequential manner: TCN is performed on input first and LSTM is followed to refine temporal representations before feeding into final-level prediction layers.

SRH

68

Figure 34: Architecture diagram TCN ­ LSTM. [author generated] The architecture is developed using PyTorch and is based on a three-stage sequential structure. In the first layer, we have a TCN branch made of five temporal blocks with channel [128, 128, 64, 64, 32] that takes as input the raw shape (batch_size,24,30). Each temporal block consists of two dilated causal convolution layers with kernel size 3, ReLU activation and 0.3 dropout, with a residual connection which employs a 1x1 convolution for dimension matching when input-output channel sizes are different. Both dilation ratios follow the classical exponential schedule of 1, 2, 4, 8 and 16 for all five blocks. As opposed to the TCN baseline in Section 4.4.2, where a single

SRH

69

time step was extracted, the TCN branch retains the full temporal length here and is output shaped (batch, 24, 32), still maintaining sequential form going into LSTM stage.

Figure 35: Code screenshot of the TCN LSTM model definition. [author generated]

In the second stage, a two-layer 128-hidden-sized LSTM with inter-layer dropout of 0.3 ingests the 32-dimensional TCN representation along all 24-time steps. The TCN features are used as input sequence for an LSTM layer which outputs a hidden state at every position, yet only the output at the final time step is kept and run through dropout before flowing into the prediction head. This enables the LSTM to learn sequential transitions over the TCN-extracted features that may capture dependencies which the convolutional structure cannot represent. In the third step, a prediction head with 3 linear layers (128 to 64 to 1) map the LSTM output to a single demand. ReLU activations and dropout of 0.3 are added after the first two layers. The total model has 484,641 parameters that can be trained and constitutes the biggest architecture used in this thesis.

4.4.4 TCN-GRU-Attention
The TCN-GRU-Attention is an extension of the sequential hybrid from Section 4.4.3 where we replace the LSTM by a GRU and add a multi-head attention layer between the recurrent output and prediction head layers. As mentioned in 2.1.4, the GRU provides a similar modeling capability as the LSTM with less parameters by combining the forget and input gates into an update gate instead of modulating them separately (Equation 2.1.9 to Equation 2.1.12). The use of multi-head attention as defined in Equations 2.1.15 and 2.1.16 makes it possible to selectively weight which time steps in the recurrent output

SRH

72

sequence should be emphasized for prediction, instead of only considering the last hidden state influence.
Following this, the model is implemented as a four-stage rolling pipeline written in PyTorch. In the first block, a 3-temporal-block TCN with channel size [64, 64, 32] is applied to raw input of shape (batch, 24, 30). It has a shallower and more narrow convolutional backbone compared to the five-block TCN in TCN-LSTM (dilations of 1, 2, 4 in the three blocks). Each temporal block consists of two dilated causal convolutions with kernel size 3, ReLU activations and dropout of 0.3, followed by a residual connection with 1x1 convolution for dimension matching. The TCN branch preserves the full temporal dimension in its output level generating a (batch, 24, 32) tensor as input to the following GRU stage.

Figure 37: Architecture diagram of the TCN-GRU-Attention model. [author generated]

SRH

73

Figure 38: Code screenshot of the TCN_GRU_Attention model definition. [author generated]

In the second stage, we use a two-layer GRU network with hidden size 128, and inter-layer dropout of 0.3 to read the 32-dimensional TCN features at all 24-time steps to output a full sequence (batch, 24,128). The choice of GRU over LSTM in this architecture is motivated by its lower parameter overhead, which partially offsets the additional parameters introduced by the attention layer. In the third stage, self-attention is applied on the entire output sequence of GRU with the scaled dot product attention mechanism given in Equation 2.1.15. This is implemented with the Multi head attention module of PyTorch, with 4 attention heads and a 128-dimensional embedding size (so each head operates on a subspace of dimension 32). Query, key and value matrices are constructed from the GRU output(self-attention) to take pair-wise relevance scores across all 24-time positions. A residual connection adds the attention output to the GRU sequence, after which a layer normalization and dropout of 0.3 are performed.

SRH

74

This post-attention normalisation avoids training instability by allowing the attention refinement to serve as an additive correction to recurrent features rather than fully replacing them. Post attention, we take only the representation from the last time step and pass it through dropout before sending it to a prediction head. The prediction head includes three linear layers with 64, 32 and 1 units with ReLU activations and a 0.3 dropout after the first two layers. The architecture contains a total of 294,177 trainable parameters.

4.4.5 TCN-CBAM-LSTM
The TCN-CBAM-LSTM is the main novel architectural part of this thesis. This architecture incorporates the Convolutional Block Attention Module (CBAM) introduced by Woo et al. [17] for computer vision, directly into the temporal convolutional backbone of a TCN- LSTM model. To the best of the author's research this combination, has never been used for bike-sharing demand prediction. CBAM works through two consecutive attention mechanisms, channel attention that learns to emphasize which feature maps contain the most useful information, and spatial attention that tells how important each temporal position within those maps is.
The channel attention mechanism compresses the temporal dimension of each feature channel using both global average pooling and global max pooling. The two pooled representations are passed through a shared two-layer MLP with a reduction ratio of 8, and the outputs are summed and activated by a sigmoid function to produce channelwise attention weights. Spatial attention module pools over all channels at each time position, concatenates the two pooling's together and performs a convolution with kernel size 7.

4.4.6 LSTM-XGBoost
The LSTM-XGBoost model contrasts to the end-to-end neural network approach used in the prior models by using a two-stage method for ensembling. Model-based boosting for XGBoost, which focuses on modelling non-linear feature interaction by regularised gradient boosting objective, is detailed in Section 2.1.7. However, each sample in this method is processed independently since every single piece of data lacks temporal order information. the LSTM encodes sequential dependencies through its gating mechanism (figures 2.1.3 to 2.1.8), yet it operates within a fixed-dimensional output space. The two-stages design leverages the advantages of both, learning temporal feature representation from raw sequences by LSTM and performing final prediction with both learned temporal features and original engineered features by XGBoost.
In stage one, 24-step input sequences are processed through an LSTM feature extractor. The extractor is a two-layer LSTM with hidden size 128 and inter-layer dropout 0.3, followed by a dense layer to only map from the last hidden state (dimension 128) to dimension 64 with ReLU activation and dropout. This LSTM is trained together with a temporary regression head (one linear layer), using MSE loss and Adam with learning rate 0.001 for 50 epochs. During testing, the regression head is removed and the 64dimensional layer output is used as learned temporal representation. The LSTM encoder has 222,272 trainable parameters and was trained for 28 epochs with early stopping patience of 15.

The Hybrid model with the best performance is the Multi-Scale TCN-LSTM, achieving a testing R-squared of 88.83% with just 92,849 parameters the most compact hybrid model. The crucial architectural novelty lies in the adoption of three parallel TCN

5.1 Evaluation Methodology
All models were tested under the strict one-step-ahead temporal protocol specified in Section 3.4. The training set consists of the initial 80 per cent of the time series (December 2017 to mid-September 2018) whereas the test set includes the last 20 per cent (mid-September to November of that year), without temporal overlap between each partition. Each model takes in a 24-hour window as input and outputs one scalar prediction for the hour that follows. Notably, all information about the target hour stays out of input in any pre-processing, feature engineering and training step.
Several studies on the Seoul Bike Sharing dataset have been published. This work follows a protocol that is a slight extension of work undertaken by several previous research over the same data. An R-square of 0.95 with their CUBIST model was reported by Sathishkumar and Cho [7] but the evaluation allowed the model access to weather observations from the same hour as that being predicted, making it an interpolation rather than forecasting problem. Aghe [40] reported 89.56 per cent R-squared in the best performing stacking ensemble, however this was evaluated using random train-test split as opposed to a temporal split and thus risks information leakage due to adjacent hours appearing in both training and test folds. In contrast, the current work guarantees that the

SRH

91

test set is future with respect to training data, yielding accuracy figures that genuinely reflect performance in operational deployments.
For each model, three complementary metrics are reported. The R-squared value shows us how much of the variance in hourly demand is explained by the model. RMSE (root mean squared error) measures prediction error in the original, bikes per hour unit and heavily penalises large deviations compared to small ones, which is operationally important since underpredicted demand at peak hours have more devastating consequences than small errors at slack periods. MAE treats all errors equally regardless of magnitude. The ratio of RMSE to MAE is a diagnostic for error concentration when the two are close, errors are evenly distributed; when RMSE far exceeds MAE the model makes large outlier error.

5.3 Comparative Analysis
The results in Table 10 allow for comparison across three axes: internal versus hybrid performance; parameter efficiency; and generalisation behaviour.
The first one that pops out is the general stronger performance of hybrid architectures compared to single baselines. The weakest hybrid (TCN-CBAM-LSTM at 84.37 per cent) still outperforms the top individual model (TCN at 75.69 per cent) to by 8.68 percentage points, and the strongest hybrid by 13.14 percentage points. This gap verifies the hypothesis of Section 2.6 that complementary architecture benefit from mixing multiple modelling paradigms and improve performance over any single architecture alone.
Though, parameter efficiency does not scale linearly with the performance. The TCN-LSTM has the largest architecture at 484,641 parameters yet this model achieves only fourth place. The Multi-Scale TCN-LSTM, on the other hand achieves the most accuracy, requiring the least hybrid parameters (92,849), with a model size approximately five times smaller than the TCN-LSTM while providing four percentage points more Rsquared. This indicates that architecture design, particularly the multi-scale convolutional structure, is more important than model capacity for this forecast task.

5.4 Error Analysis
Aggregate metrics provide an overall ranking but do not reveal where and when each model fails. To understand the error characteristics of the best-performing architecture, the prediction errors of the Multi-Scale TCN-LSTM are analysed across temporal and weather-related dimensions.
The predicted-versus-actual plot shows strong alignment along the diagonal for demand values below approximately 1,500 bikes per hour. Above this threshold, the model systematically underpredicts, a pattern consistent with the right-skewed distribution of the target variable noted in Section 3.1. Extreme peak-hour demand events

SRH

96

are rare in the training data, leaving the model with insufficient examples to learn these high-demand regimes accurately.
When errors are disaggregated by hour of day, the largest absolute errors concentrate during the morning (07:00­09:00) and evening (17:00­19:00) commuting peaks, precisely the periods of highest operational importance. During night-time hours (23:00­05:00), where demand is low and stable, prediction errors are negligible. This pattern is shared across all seven architectures, though the hybrid models reduce peakhour errors substantially compared to the individual baselines.
Weather conditions also influence error magnitude. Predictions during periods of heavy rainfall or rapid temperature changes exhibit higher residuals than those under stable weather. This is expected, since sudden weather shifts cause abrupt demand changes that the 24-hour look-back window may not fully anticipate, particularly when such events are infrequent in the training period which covers only a single year of observations.
The RMSE-to-MAE ratio for the Multi-Scale TCN-LSTM is 1.45 (204.24 / 141.27), confirming the presence of occasional large outlier errors that inflate RMSE relative to MAE. By comparison, the LSTM baseline has a ratio of 1.36, indicating that while its overall performance is weaker, its errors are more uniformly distributed.

5.5 Data Leakage Investigation
Test R˛ values more than 95 per cent on the TCN baseline during early model development stages were flagged as suspicious, and unachievable when considering the stringent one-step-ahead evaluation protocol. Upon investigation, it turned out that the target column, referred to both as "Rented Bike Count" and as "target" in the preprocessed dataset, was wrongly included with the input features. So, the answer was part of what the model was taking in, making predicting the answer a nearly trivial identity mapping.
Once this was recognized, a safety system was added into all seven of the training scripts. Each script also explicitly excludes both names of the target variable from being included in input tensors if either is detected among the input features and raises an exception for either case. See Figure 53 for the relevant code. All results reported in this thesis were generated after the introduction of this safeguard, and no model was given access to the target variable at any point when constructing inputs.

Such situations highlight a more general risk in time-series forecasting pipelines where the target variable can take different names or even be derived features after preprocessing. In this case, the duplication happened because the feature engineering script added a "target" column to the original "Rented Bike Count", as needed for model training, but accidentally managed to leave both in Final CSV files. The takeaway from these pragmatic issues is that manual inspection alone is not sufficient to prevent such leakage, and that strong automated safeguards are needed particularly in the presence of multiple scripts and intermediate data files involved in the pipeline. With these safeguards confirmed, the following section discusses the broader implications of the evaluation results.

5.6 Discussion

Analysis Results Provide Evidence for Three Key Findings First, for hybrid architectures, we see that they always outperform commonly predicted baselines for hourly bike-shared demand prediction under one-step-ahead setting. The minimum hybrid R-squared (84.37 per cent) is more than eight percentage points greater than the best individual model, suggesting that complementary modelling paradigms can be

SRH

98

combined to capture temporal patterns not extracted with either convolutional or recurrent networks in isolation.
Second, the architecture design plays a more important role in prediction accuracy than model size. Of the two TCN-LSTM approaches, the Multi-Scale TCN-LSTM achieves the highest R-squared value with considerably less parameters than the TCN-LSTM (the former has five times less parameters for four percentage points more accuracy). The critical design element seems to be the multi-kernel structure that processes the input at three temporal resolutions in parallel. As the ablation result confirms (88.83% drops only to 88.43% when the LSTM component is removed), suggesting that predictive performance is driven largely by a convolutional backbone instead of recurrent refinement.
Third, while being fifth ranked, the TCN-CBAM-LSTM proves that channel-spatial attention modules from computer vision space can transfer to temporal demand forecasting with little parameter overhead (0.6 per cent). This combination has not been used for bike-sharing prediction as far as we know. Possibly the relatively low rank of the matrix compared to other hybrids, indicates that cross-channel and across-features attention is not as useful as temporal processing at different time scales for this dataset (although the approach may be more helpful in datasets with higher-dimensional or more diverse feature spaces).
Several limitations warrant acknowledgment. The assessment is limited to a single city and one year of data, so the relative rankings of architectures may not generalise to systems built in different usage patterns or climates. All the individual baselines are overfitting to a large degree, with the LSTM gap reaching nearly 11 percentage points, which can probably be alleviated to some extent by stronger regularisation or improved data augmentation techniques. All models also underperform during peak commuting hours and rapid weather transitions, which is precisely when accurate predictions are most operationally useful. Further work is still open into addressing this peak-hour weakness, possibly through asymmetric loss functions that penalise underprediction more than over-predictions.

Chapter 6: Conclusion and Future Work

This chapter closes the thesis by synthesizing results from each chapter into a comprehensive view of what was done and what was learned. The main goal of this work was to evaluate the potential of deep learning architectures for predicting hourly demand (usage) for all bikes in each public bike-sharing system, specifically Seoul's public bikesharing system using UCI The Seoul Bike Sharing Dataset as input data. We designed as well as implemented two individual baselines and five hybrid combinations of them into seven architectures and evaluated under a strict one-step-ahead temporal evaluation protocol. The results show that the hybrid architecture outperforms each individual model consistently and to a greater degree, with Multi-Scale TCN - LSTM reaching the highest test R-squared of 88.83 per cent while remaining most parameter-efficient (92,849 parameters). The chapter is structured as follows: Section 6.1 summarise each of the chapters' contributions; Section 6.2 tackles each research question in turn; and Section 6.3 presents limitations on this work as well as avenues for further research.
6.1 Summary of Contributions
Chapter 1 established the operational motivation for precise short-term demand forecasting in urban bike-sharing systems, framed four research questions around the capability of deep learning, the role of attention, the comparative performance of hybrid architectures, and architectural trade-offs, and outlined the CRISP-DM methodology that structured the study.
Chapter 2 surveyed the theoretical foundations and related literature, covering LSTM, GRU, TCN, multi-head attention, CBAM, and XGBoost, and reviewed prior forecasting work on the Seoul Bike Sharing dataset. The review identified a persistent gap; most prior studies assess only one or two architectures in isolation and employ evaluation protocols same-time prediction or random train-test splits that inflate reported accuracy relative to operational conditions.
Chapter 3 presented the dataset and methodological blueprint. The UCI Seoul Bike Sharing dataset, comprising 8,760 hourly observations across a full calendar year, was characterised in terms of its distributional properties and temporal dynamics. The chapter specified the 80/20 chronological train-test split, the one-step-ahead prediction protocol, and the three-evaluation metrics applied uniformly across all models.

SRH

102

Chapter 4 documented the complete implementation. A two-phase feature engineering pipeline expanded 14 raw variables into 64 candidate features and subsequently reduced the set to 30 optimised features through Pearson correlation analysis, eliminating 34 high-redundancy pairs across six feature categories. All seven model architectures were then implemented, trained, and configured, covering the range from the individual LSTM and TCN baselines through to the five hybrid designs. The chapter also documented the discovery and systematic resolution of a data leakage issue encountered during early development.
Chapter 5 evaluated all seven architectures under identical conditions and reported the full results. The two individual baselines were outperformed by every hybrid model by a substantial margin. The Multi-Scale TCN-LSTM emerged as the bestperforming and most parameter-efficient architecture, and an ablation study isolated the contribution of its multi-scale convolutional backbone from the recurrent refinement stage. Results were additionally contextualised against prior work on the same dataset, with the methodological differences in evaluation protocols carefully acknowledged.
6.2 Answers to Research Questions
RQ1: Is it possible to use a deep learning model to better describe the intricate nonlinear temporal patterns of Seoul bike-sharing demand, offering accurate hourly predictions?
The individual baseline experiments confirm that deep learning models can capture meaningful structure in Seoul's bike-sharing demand signal, though the degree to which they do so depends heavily on the architectural complexity employed. The LSTM baseline, comprising three stacked recurrent layers, achieved a test R-squared of 66.32 per cent with an RMSE of 354.58 bikes per hour a result that demonstrates the model has learned the broad demand rhythm but leaves a substantial portion of variance unexplained. The TCN baseline performed somewhat better at 75.69 per cent R-squared and an RMSE of 301.28, suggesting that the dilated convolutional approach is more effective than pure recurrence at extracting local temporal features from the 24-hour input window. Both models, however, exhibit a pronounced generalisation gap; the LSTM's training R-squared exceeds 75 per cent, implying that it has overfit to seasonal and temporal patterns present in the training period that do not transfer reliably to the heldout test months. The TCN's gap is smaller at approximately 9.8 percentage points, yet its

SRH

103

absolute test performance remains well below what would be required for operational deployment.
These findings suggest a qualified answer to the research question. Deep learning models can unquestionably extract nonlinear temporal patterns from the data performing substantially above what a naďve mean predictor would achieve but individual architectures alone are insufficient for high-accuracy hourly forecasting on this dataset. The complexity of bike-sharing demand, which is shaped simultaneously by commuting cycles, weather transitions, seasonal effects, and calendar events, appears to exceed what a single modelling paradigm can fully represent. This observation directly motivates the hybrid architecture investigation pursued in the preceding chapter of the thesis.

RQ2: Is it useful to integrate attention mechanisms in hybrid architectures of forecasting?
Two attention-augmented architectures were evaluated: the TCN-GRU-Attention, which applies a four-head scaled dot-product attention module over the sequential output of a GRU layer, and the TCN-CBAM-LSTM, which incorporates channel and spatial attention from the Convolutional Block Attention Module between selected TCN layers before a recurrent refinement stage. Their results are compared against three nonattention hybrids the TCN-LSTM, the LSTM-XGBoost, and the Multi-Scale TCN-LSTM under identical experimental conditions.
The TCN-GRU-Attention achieves a test R-squared of 85.58 per cent and an RMSE of 231.98, ranking third among the seven architectures. The TCN-CBAM-LSTM reaches 84.37 per cent R-squared and an RMSE of 241.59, placing fifth. Both attentionaugmented models comfortably outperform the individual baselines, and the TCN-GRUAttention delivers a minor improvement over the non-attention Hybrid TCN-LSTM (84.75 per cent), suggesting that temporal attention over sequential features provides a measurable benefit for this forecasting task. The TCN-CBAM-LSTM's result, while lower in absolute terms, is notable for what it achieves with minimal additional complexity; its CBAM module requires only 2,093 parameters 0.6 per cent of the model's total parameter count yet still enables the architecture to match or exceed several larger hybrid designs.
However, the overall ranking reveals an important limitation; neither attentionbased architecture reaches the accuracy of the Multi-Scale TCN-LSTM (88.83 per cent), which contains no attention mechanism whatsoever. This indicates that, for the specific

SRH

104

characteristics of hourly bike-sharing demand, architectural design choices around multiscale temporal processing outweigh the benefit of explicit attention-based weighting. Attention mechanisms are therefore useful in this context they improve upon simpler hybrids and, in the case of TCN-GRU-Attention, contribute a degree of interpretability through the attention weight distribution but they are not the primary driver of forecasting accuracy. The answer to RQ2 is thus affirmative but qualified; attention integration provides measurable gains for certain hybrid configurations, yet the design of the convolutional and recurrent backbone remains the more decisive factor for predictive performance.

RQ3: Can hybrid architectures based on a mixture of neural network paradigms improve the performance of each paradigm by itself in short-term bike demand prediction?
The results provide an unambiguous affirmative answer. All five hybrid architectures the Hybrid TCN-LSTM, TCN-GRU-Attention, TCN-CBAM-LSTM, LSTMXGBoost, and Multi-Scale TCN-LSTM achieve higher test R-squared values than either individual baseline, or the gap is substantial rather than marginal. The weakest hybrid, the TCN-CBAM-LSTM at 84.37 per cent, exceeds the strongest individual model, the TCN at 75.69 per cent, by 8.68 percentage points. The best hybrid, the Multi-Scale TCN-LSTM at 88.83 per cent, surpasses the best individual by 13.14 percentage points an improvement that translates to a reduction in RMSE from 301.28 to 204.24 bikes per hour, a difference of operational significance for fleet rebalancing decisions.
The consistency of this result across five structurally diverse hybrid architectures spanning sequential TCN-to-LSTM composition, two-stage ensemble learning with XGBoost, attention-augmented recurrence, and parallel multi-scale convolution strengthens the conclusion considerably. No single integration strategy is uniquely responsible; the benefit of hybridisation appears to arise from a general principle that combining complementary modelling paradigms allows each component to compensate for the limitations of the other. Recurrent components capture sequential dependencies across longer time horizons that convolutional filters alone may not span, whilst convolutional components provide efficient local feature extraction that reduces the burden placed on the recurrent stage. The LSTM-XGBoost result (86.67 per cent) further illustrates that this principle extends beyond pure neural hybrids; even a two-stage

SRH

105

pipeline in which a neural network extracts latent features that are then passed to a gradient boosted tree achieves substantially higher accuracy than either modelling approach could reach independently on this dataset.

RQ4: What is the best architectural configuration for bike demand forecasting and what trade-offs exist among prediction accuracy, model complexity, and computational efficiency?
Across all metrics and evaluation criteria, the Multi-Scale TCN-LSTM emerges as the best-performing architecture. It achieves a test R-squared of 88.83 per cent, an RMSE of 204.24 bikes per hour, and an MAE of 141.27, whilst using only 92,849 parameters the fewest among all five hybrid models. The architecture processes each 24-hour input sequence through three parallel dilated convolutional branches with kernel sizes of 2, 3, and 5, capturing short-, medium-, and long-range temporal dependencies simultaneously, before a two-layer LSTM refines the concatenated multi-scale representation into a final hourly demand estimate. An ablation study conducted in Chapter 5 confirmed that the multi-scale convolutional backbone is the primary driver of this performance; the TCNonly variant, evaluated without the LSTM refinement stage, achieves 88.43 per cent Rsquared, just 0.40 percentage points below the full model. The LSTM stage therefore contributes a measurable but modest gain whilst consuming 82 per cent of the model's parameter budget, a trade-off that is architecturally informative even if the absolute accuracy cost of removing it is small.
The trade-off analysis across all seven architectures reveals that parameter count is a poor proxy for predictive performance. The TCN-LSTM, with 484,641 parameters more than five times the size of the Multi-Scale TCN-LSTM achieves only 84.75 per cent R-squared, placing fourth. Conversely, the LSTM-XGBoost ensemble (86.67 per cent, second place) derives its advantage not from sheer parameter count but from its twostage design, which delegates sequential pattern recognition to a neural feature extractor and nonlinear interaction modelling to a gradient boosted tree. For applications where interpretability is a priority, the TCN-GRU-Attention (85.58 per cent, third place) offers a practical alternative; its attention weight distribution provides a post-hoc indicator of which timesteps in the input window the model draws upon most heavily, a property absent from the other architectures. The TCN-CBAM-LSTM, ranking fifth at 84.37 per cent, represents the novel architectural contribution of this thesis the first application of CBAM channel-

SRH

106

spatial attention to bike-sharing demand forecasting and its result suggests that crosschannel feature recalibration, whilst beneficial in computer vision contexts, provides less advantage for this particular temporal forecasting task than multi-scale temporal processing does. In summary, the Multi-Scale TCN-LSTM is recommended as the preferred architecture when accuracy and parameter efficiency are the primary criteria; the TCN-GRU-Attention is preferable when interpretability is required; and the TCNCBAM-LSTM stands as the exploratory novel contribution whose relative performance may differ on datasets with higher feature dimensionality or greater cross-channel complexity.
6.3 Limitations and Future Work
Before outlining possible future research directions, several limitations of the present study should be acknowledged. For example, the models were trained on data from a single city Seoul for a single calendar year, which vitiates the generalisability of the architectural rankings we report here. The demand signal for bike-sharing is influenced by local infrastructure, cultural commuting practices, and climate that will differ significantly between cities; a model setup optimized for Seoul's demand signal might not be as performant in other cities with different use patterns, denser station networks or more variable seasonal performance. Extending the experimental design to datasets from several cities or training a single model across cities and assessing transfer would greatly strengthen the conclusions drawn through comparative analysis.
A related limitation is the level of spatial aggregation. This thesis focuses on system-wide hourly demand predictions for all seven models, while real-world rebalancing operations needs station-level forecasts of when and where bikes are to be moved. The system-wide formulation is useful as a research baseline it cleanly isolates the temporal modelling problem but does not capture the full operational challenge. Station-level demand is much sparser, and more irregular compared to the aggregate signal, and the relationship between stations creates a spatial structure that is not modelled by any of the architectures explored here.
The search for hyperparameters was also inevitably constrained. Since all training was done on CPU hardware, the number of configurations explored per reasonable time budget is limited. The final architectures are highly optimised configurations but not exhaustively so; broader search or use of automated hyperparameter optimisation

SRH

107

methods such as Bayesian optimisation could yield further gains, especially for the individual baseline models, whose generalisation gaps indicate that regularisation settings were likely not fully tuned.
Such limitations highlight directly fruitful avenues for future work. The most natural extension is going from system level to station level prediction, a likely need for graphbased architectures like Graph Convolutional Networks or Spatial-Temporal Graph Convolutional Networks which can represent the dependency structure between stations both spatially and temporally in terms of demand evolution. A second high-value opportunity is multi-step forecasting; all models described in this thesis predict only singlehour out, yet operational planning relies on forecasts at three-, six-and twelve-hour horizons. Multi-step prediction also brings extra uncertainty that current architectures cannot quantify, establishing probabilistic forecasting methods for example Monte Carlo dropout, deep ensembles or conformal prediction being expected next steps to produce actionable confidence intervals with point estimates. A third avenue relates to transferability; training the best-performing architecture on data from several cities, then evaluating zero-shot or fine-tuned performance on new cities would help determine whether learned temporal representations are specific to Seoul or capture more general dynamics of urban demand. A systematic analysis of the attention weight distributions generated by the TCN-GRU-Attention model was not conducted in this thesis; having performed feature attribution with SHAP Based models or visualising attention across time steps and weather conditions would have increased interpretability, possibly indicating which demand drivers are most heavily relied on by the model under different operating conditions.
