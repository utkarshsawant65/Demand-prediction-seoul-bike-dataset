P115: [Normal] 'Bike-sharing system\u2002is one of prominent elements in the urban transportation which provides sustainable and convenient solution for private vehicles and conventional public transit. Bike-sharing programmes have expanded rapidly across cities worldwide, serving millions of users and broadening the range of sustainable transport options with a wider alternative to cars,\u2002saving greenhouse-gas emissions and promoting healthier lifestyles. But to optimize these systems it is important to know\u2002when and where people will demand bikes.'
P116: [Normal] 'In past few years, cities such as\u2002Seoul have invested on developing their public bike-sharing infrastructure. Seoul’s system is spread out over hundreds of stations, carrying millions and a wide range\u2002of people who use it daily to get to work or school or for recreation. Demand for bikes fluctuates considerably over\u2002the course of each day, as a function of factors such as weather and time of year, day of week, holiday season or time of day. In the morning and evening rush-hour, demand rises steeply as\u2002people go back and forth to work, but on weekends or holidays the usage pattern looks quite different. Ridership also declines during adverse weather conditions if there is rain or snow, and some parts of town can just seem too hot or too cold to get on a bike. This fact makes demand prediction a difficult problem, the\u2002system needs to consider several features that interact among themselves and that evolve over time.'
P117: [Normal] 'Effective bike-sharing\u2002system relies on precise short-term demand prediction. Inaccurate prediction results in wasted resources and reduced user satisfaction. With the rapid expansion of bike-sharing systems, there is an increasing requirement on effective\u2002and accurate data-driven demand forecasting.'

Through historical bike-rental,\u2002weather and other relevant features from the UCI Seoul Bike Sharing dataset we study accuracy of different model architectures and how best to predict demand. The analysis provides a detailed understanding of the most important characteristics influencing demand for bike-sharing and\u2002the benefits of hybrid model predictions leveraging multiple model combinations.'

P181: [Heading 2] '2.1 Time Series Forecasting'
P182: [Normal] 'It is the growing collection of large historical data in many scientific and practical areas that has triggered the demand for\u2002adequate and efficient forecasting methodologies to extract the stochastic dependence between past patterns and future values, when provided with past observations [8]. Over the past few decades, time series modeling and forecasting have become more and more popular in topics including transportation, energy, medical science, climate science and urban studies with many successful applications in each of above mentioned domains [8][9][10]. When it\u2002comes to different sectors, a proper method should be chosen and applied into the raw time series data in order to get more accurate predictions. The basic aim of time series modelling is to analyse historical data, identify any significant pattern(s) and deduce a model that can be subsequently used for predicting future points by\u2002extracting some useful information from the past observations.'
P183: [Normal] 'Time series can be described as a sequence of clearly ordered data points collected at regular time intervals\u2002[8]. It is a staple technique in time series analysis, not only allowing to investigate the temporal aspects of some dataset but also to venture a forecasted\u2002future based on this temporal behavior. For example, in the field of city-wide transportation, hourly demand data for bike-sharing exhibits strong daily and weekly seasonality due to commuter cycles as well as longer\u2002term behavior associated with weather pattern changes [7].'
P184: [Normal] 'However, time series data often trend or are seasonal or cyclical and that\u2002means the relationship between input variables to the model and predicted output changes with time. Observations are not independent since they follow each other, due to\u2002the successive nature of the data. So, selecting time-lag discourse value is\u2002so important to find data subsets that have a high correlation in among the dependent and independent variables. The generated predictions are then more accurate and precise\u2002[10]. Moreover, external influencing factors such as weather effects and holidays or special events may add\u2002additional sudden change in the underlying patterns; and thus make the prediction task much more difficult.'
P185: [Normal] 'For the planning and\u2002decision making tasks that would be performed in any applied context, accurate prediction is crucial. To tackle these challenges, various forecasting methods have been proposed including traditional linear statistical methods such as Autoregressive Integrated Moving Average models (ARIMA) [11], gradient-boosting-based approaches such as eXtreme Gradient Boosting (XGBoost) [12], and deep learning architectures such\u2002as Recurrent Neural Networks (RNN), Long Short-Term Memory (LSTM) networks [13], Temporal Convolutional Networks (TCN) [14] and attention-based model s[15]. There have also been very recent work on using transformer architectures for time series forecasting [9] with notable\u2002success. But there is no preferred method\u2002which would be appropriate for every time series and prediction problem, and the selection of an appropriate approach still depends much on the nature of data, horizon to forecast, and application context.'
P186: [Heading 3] '2.1.1 Supervised Learning and Deep Learning'
P187: [Normal] 'Machine learning\u2002(ML) assumes and form a subset of the field of artificial intelligence that enables algorithms to learn from data and experience and find patterns so that, given an independent input, predictions can be made without extensive human intervention [18]. Unlike conventional algorithms, which adhere to a set of explicitly programmed rules for\u2002making decisions, intelligent machine learning algorithms learn directly from data using an iterative methodology and they discover information that is valuable from amongst voluminous amounts [19]. With more data and more diversity of\u2002applicable scenarios available, machine learning systems can be trained much better to get the right answer.'
P188: [Normal] 'Machine learning algorithms can usually be categorized into four main categories supervised, unsupervised,\u2002semi-supervised and reinforcement learning [18]. In this thesis, we focus on supervised learning as all\u2002models are trained on labelled historical data, i.e. the input features and associated target values are provided.'
P189: [Normal] ''
P190: [Caption] 'Figure 2.1.1.1 Overview of a supervised machine learning pipeline for training and prediction [26]'
P191: [Normal] 'Supervised machine learning seeks a mapping function from\u2002an input feature space X to the corresponding output labels Y, given a set of labeled samples. It tries\u2002to find the function Y = f(X) (where X is input, and Y is output), by adjusting the trainable parameters of a given model in such a way that the loss on training data is minimized. After model parameters are well-tuned, the model trained is employed to predict new data\u2002which has not been seen before [18].'
P192: [Normal] 'Deep learning is a subfield of machine learning which is based on artificial\u2002neural networks with multiple layers, where higher levels in the hierarchy represent more abstract features, and is used to model complex patterns through its deep structure [19]. Unlike conventional machine learning methods which often involve the manual feature engineering process,\u2002deep learning models can automatically learn the informative and non-linear relations from data by means of multiple stacked layers. Each layer in a deep neural network learns higher level representations depending on the weights of its previous\u2002layers, which allows the model to pick out patterns that we may not be able to describe or define ourselves.'
P193: [Normal] 'In the realm of time series prediction, approaches based on deep learning have shown extraordinary performance compared to standard technologies from the fields of statistics and\u2002machine learning when handling big data that encapsulate complex chronological relations, non-linear interdependencies and combined input variables [9]. Structures such as Long Short-Term Memory (LSTM) networks [13], Temporal Convolutional Networks\u2002(TCN) [14] and Gated Recurrent Units (GRU) [16] have been specially crafted or adapted for sequential data and have outperformed other techniques on a diverse set of forecasting problems [5]. Together with attention mechanisms and hybrids, these architectures will serve as a cornerstone for the models investigated in this thesis and are described\u2002in greater details in the next subsections.'
P194: [Heading 3] '2.1.2 Recurrent Neural Network (RNN)'
P195: [Normal] 'An RNN is a type of artificial neural network, these networks are designed to process sequential data with internal hidden states capturing information from previous time steps\u2002[20]. While regular feedforward neural networks have connections\u2002that flow one way from input to output, RNNs also bring signals back to the network (that is why they are called recurrent). This makes them especially relevant in circumstances in which we care about the order in the data, as it is\u2002often the case for time series forecasting, natural language processing, and speech recognition.'
P196: [Normal] 'An RNN architecture is simply a chain\u2002of repeating neural network modules. The network\u2002gets the current input and previous hidden state at each time step, computes them together, outputs result along with updating current hidden state in next time step [20]. This allows the network to in theory capture long-range temporal dependencies\u2002over the full length of the input sequence.'
P197: [Normal] 'However, in practice, many RNNs\u2002exhibit a well-documented problem called the vanishing gradient problem [21]. Gradients are passed backward in time and\u2002used to update the weights during training. As the length of sequences grows those gradients are repeatedly multiplied by some small value\u2002and they shrink rapidly (exponentially) to zero. This makes learning of long-term dependencies in the network very hard, as the\u2002effective trace of earlier time-steps fades away quickly after learning progresses. On the other hand, in some cases, gradients may explode that is\u2002called exploding gradient problem [21].'
P198: [Normal] ''
P199: [Caption] 'Figure 2.1.2.1: Unrolled RNN architecture illustrating how the same recurrent unit processes sequential inputs over multiple time steps [23]'
P200: [Normal] 'Not withstanding those restrictions, RNNs have received\u2002a fair amount of attention in a range of fields with optimistic performances. In traffic forecasting, Ma\u2002et al. [31] presented an RNN model for traffic speed prediction in a long-term horizon, and they showed that the Recurrent Neural Network (RNN) was more effective at capturing non-linear complex patterns of traffic flow than models\u2002with parametric forms. Regarding\u2002a short-term transportation demand study, used a simple RNN model to forecast station-level bike-sharing demand, and discovered that a simplified recurrent network could exceed traditional time series techniques such as ARIMA in presence of\u2002non-linear usage patterns from across multiple stations. These experiments show the promise of recurrent architectures for temporal prediction in transportation\u2002tasks, but they also reveal that more sophisticated variants are needed to model long-range data dependencies.'
P201: [Heading 3] '2.1.3 Long Short-Term Memory (LSTM)'
P202: [Normal] 'The Long Short-Term Memory (LSTM) was proposed by Hochreiter and Schmidhuber [13] in 1997, motivated by the\u2002vanishing gradient problem affecting standard RNNs. The long, short term memory\u2002(LSTM) model introduces a dedicated memory cell and gating scheme, which enables the network to retain, modify or forget information for distant time-steps. This structure enables\u2002the LSTMs to model short-term and long-term temporal dependencies, and as such are one of the most popular architectures for time series forecasting problems [9].'
P203: [Normal] ''
P204: [Caption] 'Figure 2.1.3.1 LSTM Network [24]'
P205: [Normal] 'At a basic level, the LSTM architecture is comprised of four interacting "entities", namely Memory Cells, Input Gate, Forget Gate\u2002and Output Gate. The forget\u2002gate decides which information about the previous cell state should be thrown away upon the combination with the current input and last hidden state. The input gate decides what new information should be stored to the cell\u2002state. The cell state itself can serve\u2002as a conveyer belt to carry relevant information across time steps and thus counteract the loss of information with little degradation. The output gate determines\u2002which part of the cell state will be exposed as a hidden state output at the current time step [13]. Now the network can learn which pieces of information are important\u2002to remember and which can be forgotten, mitigating the long-term dependency issue that traditional RNNs have problems. The gating mechanism is formalised in Figure 2.1.3.2, where σ denotes the sigmoid activation function, ⊙ represents the element-wise product, and [h_{t-1}, x_t] is the concatenation of the previous hidden state with the current input.'
P206: [Normal] ''
P207: [Normal] 'Figure 2.1.3.2: LSTM Gate Equations [13] [author generated]'
P208: [Normal] 'Since\u2002then, LSTM has been widely used in many forecasting areas including (but not limited to) transportation and urban mobility. Ma et al. [31] proposed to use an LSTM network for long-term traffic speed prediction from microwave sensors and they found that\u2002the LSTM model clearly outperformed classical approaches such as ARIMA techniques and standard feedforward neural networks in terms of grasping complex underlying non-linear traffic dynamics. Related work in bike-sharing demand prediction, Min and Jung [25] compared\u2002three models with a dataset consisting of daily data for 389 Seoul bike stations. They contrasted with classic models such as Vector\u2002Autoregression versus the plain LSTM (i.e., baseline) when modelling sequential information suggesting that LSTMs are superior for learning long-term dependencies, thus laying the groundwork for deep learning architectures and bike demand prediction.'
P209: [Normal] 'Additional confirmation of the applicability of\u2002LSTM for demand forecasting is provided by Lin et al. [33] for predicting station-level hourly\u2002demand in a large-scale bike-share system using an LSTM-based model. The LSTM model was shown to capture the intricate temporal dynamics in bike usage data and yielded better predictions than a variety of baseline approaches, especially under high\u2002demand variation (e.g., rush hours and weather-related events). Together, these studies are a testament to the powerful capacity of LSTM networks relating to capturing temporal dependencies for transportation prediction tasks and indicate that using LSTM in combination with other\u2002deep or hand-engineered mechanisms could lead to greater predictive performance.'
P210: [Heading 3] '2.1.4 Gated Recurrent Units (GRU)'
P211: [Normal] 'Gated\u2002Recurrent Unit (GRU) was proposed by Cho et al. [16] as an easier, more elegant solution to the\u2002LSTM architecture. Whereas LSTMs have three individual gates and their\u2002own cell state to facilitate the control of information flow, the GRU uses two gates instead and does not hold a separate cell state. The two gates in a GRU are\u2002called the update gate and the reset gate. Consequently, the model has fewer trained parameters compared to an LSTM, which\u2002makes for faster training and lower computational costs while still being able to capture long-range dependencies on sequential data [16].'
P212: [Normal] 'In a GRU, the update gate determines how much of the previous hidden state should be propagated to current time step, which essentially unifies functions\u2002of the forget and input gates in an LSTM. The closer the update gate towards one, the more\u2002previous information to be maintained by the network and when it approaches zero, then the newly computed candidate information is retained. The reset gate controls the degree to\u2002which we should forget the previous hidden state. When the value of reset gate is close to zero, the model in such configuration forgets past context and acts as if it were reading the first\u2002element in sequence which can be beneficial for modeling short-term dependencies where further information from distant past may not have any significant effect [18].'

P220: [Heading 3] '2.1.5 Temporal Convolutional Networks (TCN)'
P221: [Normal] 'Temporal Convolutional\u2002Networks (TCN) were proposed in Bai et al. [14] in 2018 as an alternative to state-of-the-art recurrent networks, traditionally believed to be more naturally suited for temporal based data\u2002processing. The\u2002TCN is based on three fundamental principles causal convolutions, dilated convolutions, and residual connections. These parts form a whole and\u2002allow the network to process sequential data in an efficient manner and at the same time they keep the power of capturing dependencies for wide range of time scales.'
P222: [Normal] 'Causal convolutions ensure that the output at any time step depends only on current and past inputs, thereby preventing any information leakage from the future. This property\u2002is crucial for time series forecast, as it would cause data leakage and too optimistic results if we use future data during prediction [14]. However, a drawback of traditional causality-based convolutions is that the attention over past time steps of processed data is limited by\u2002the kernel size and depth limit of the network. Dealing with long-range temporal dependencies within\u2002this constraint would either demand very deep networks or large filter sizes, both of which entail computational and optimization difficulties.'
P223: [Normal] 'Dilated\u2002convolutions were created to overcome this issue. Dilated convolutions increase the size of network’s receptive field without increasing complexity or\u2002computational load by introducing gaps between filter elements. If the expansion factor is increased exponentially (e.g., with rates of 1, 2, 4, 8 and 16) across the layers then we can get a very large receptive\u2002field size even for a small number of layers [14]. This enables the TCN to both capture fine-grained short-term patterns, e.g., hourly swings in demand for bikes, and longer-term dependencies (weekly or seasonal\u2002cycles).'

P226: [Normal] 'Residual connections, inspired by Zhang et al. [29] who applied them to deep image recognition networks, are also integrated into the TCN design. A residual connection enables the input to a convolutional block to bypass through the blocks and is\u2002directly added to its output, facilitating a shorter gradient path in backpropagation. This contributes to\u2002alleviating the degradation issue so that increasing the network depth in a deep architecture does not simply lead to an increased error of training but well performed [29]. P229: [Normal] 'A prominent benefit of TCN over recurrences sequences such as LSTM and GRU is the ability to compute convolutions over\u2002complete input sequences in parallel, since each output member can be calculated without any other. Recurrent instances do need to consume\u2002the sequence step by step (hidden state directly depends on the output of previous time step). P231: [Heading 3] '2.1.6 Attention Mechanisms'
P232: [Normal] 'The attention mechanism was\u2002first proposed in the field of neural machine translation by Bahdanau et al. [34], to enabling the decoder in a sequence-to-sequence model to\u2002attend selectively to different segments of the input sequence when generating each output element. Instead of reducing the whole input\u2002to a fixed-length vector, the attention mechanism computes weighted sums over all input positions where the weights were computed based on which position is most important in deciding the current prediction. This idea was later generalized and expanded by Vaswani\u2002et al. [15] in the self-attention and Transformer architecture, that have since been developed to be a ubiquitous building block across many domains such\u2002as natural language processing (NLP), computer vision, and time series Forecasting.'
P233: [Normal] 'For self-attention, we compare every time step in the input against all other\u2002time steps to calculate a set of attention weights. These weights indicate the relevance or dependence of elements on each other, enabling the\u2002model to learn long-range dependencies throughout the sequence Luong et al [35]. The multi-head attention version addresses this by performing several attention operations in parallel,\u2002each with its learned set of projection parameters [15]. The outputs\u2002of these learned attention heads are then concatenated and linearly transformed to get the final results. By utilizing multiple heads, the model can attend to information from\u2002different representation subspaces at different positions concurrently which enhances its ability to learn diverse temporal patterns in the data.'

P252: [Heading 2] '2.2 Feature Engineering for Time Series'
P253: [Normal] 'Forecasting\u2002performance is a joint function of the architecture and quality of the input features to the model. For bike-sharing demand quantity prediction, using the\u2002raw hourly counts and meteorological measurements directly cannot fully reflect the complicated patterns beneath because multiple factors are actually affecting ridership through interaction and they distribute on a different range of time scale. Feature engineering deals with this via the transformation of raw data into a more\u2002informative set of input variables, which make these patterns more readily apparent to the learning algorithm [7].'
P254: [Normal] 'The simplest is generating lag feature, which will allow the model to access previous\u2002values of target variable. There are strong temporal dependencies in the hourly demand, where 1 hour lag could capture short-term momentum, a 24 hour lag would contain daily commuting cycle and a 168-hour lag\u2002represents weekly periodicity [9]. By incorporating lag horizons at multiple time scales, the model can have simultaneous access to both the immediate past, as well as what happened n hours ago on the same day and one week ago at n-th hour of a\u2002day which is essential to capture both regular commuting patterns (due to people’s daily routine) and event driven variation. Rolling window statistics act as\u2002a complement to lag features by taking aggregate measures like the average, standard deviation over a sliding window of recent observations, smoothing short-term noise and capturing local trends in demand [10].'
P255: [Normal] 'Temporal features like hour of the day and day of week contain valuable information\u2002on what to expect in demand levels but treating them as plain integers is not representative of their cyclical nature. Hour 23 is “next to” hour 0, in the sense of being adjacent in\u2002time but maximally distant as integers, and likewise for Sunday and Monday. Cyclical encoding addresses this by representing each temporal variable using sine and cosine components which\u2002maintain circular continuity while allowing the model to learn smooth periodic patterns that spans cycle boundaries [10].'

P269: [Heading 3] '2.4.1 Traditional and Statistical Approaches'
P270: [Normal] 'The first generation of bike-sharing demand prediction studies\u2002mostly leveraged classical time series operation models and regression approaches best practices in common transportation planning activities. P320: [Normal] "The most important decision that faces one now is how to slice up one year's worth of data into training chunks and testing chunks Random splitting would be a serious mistake for this kind of problem. P336: [Heading 2] '3.3 Modelling Approach'
P337: [Normal] 'The literature review in Chapter 2 established that individual deep learning architectures, while capable of modelling temporal patterns in bike-sharing demand, face inherent limitations in predictive accuracy when applied in isolation. At the same time, the gap analysis in Section 2.6 revealed that hourly demand forecasting under strict temporal evaluation conditions remains underexplored, particularly with hybrid architectures that combine complementary modelling paradigms. This thesis addresses that gap by systematically constructing and evaluating seven distinct architectures, all operating under identical experimental conditions. Each model receives the same thirty optimised features arranged in twenty-four-hour input sequences and produces a single output: the predicted rental count for the next hour. Because every other variable is held constant, any observed difference in performance can be attributed solely to the architectural design. As introduced in Section 1.4, the overall research process follows the CRISP-DM framework [6], whose iterative stages map directly onto the structure of this thesis: Sections 3.1 and 3.2 correspond to data understanding and data preparation, this section defines the modelling strategy, Chapter 4 addresses implementation, and Chapter 5 covers evaluation.'
P338: [Normal] 'An important methodological choice relates to the target of\u2002prediction. A few previous works on the Seoul dataset particularly [7] and [40], provide calendar and weather data from\u2002the GMT hour to which the prediction is to be made, thus giving implicit access to up-to-date conditions. P339: [Normal] 'The seven architectures are organised into two tiers. The first tier comprises two individual baselines. Together, these two baselines establish a performance floor that any hybrid must surpass to justify its added complexity.'
P340: [Normal] 'The second set level includes five hybrids, the models of which were developed in\u2002order to validate a given hypothesis identified in the gap analysis presented in Section 2. P344: [Heading 2] '3.4 Evaluation Plan'
P345: [Normal] "Although the simple concept of assessing a forecast model is the process is where the idea differs what turns out to be of consequence as much as outcome itself. Don't set results by accuracy numbers reported in studies, two studies using the same dataset may report substantially different results simply because their evaluation protocols differ. In this section, the evaluation plan to be used in the article presented here is outlined and its motivation is explained. This work reports only performance numbers that come from the test set the last twenty percent of a time series that no model has seen during training. No random cross-validation is done, because random sampling disrupts the temporal ordering of observations and produces overly optimistic estimates of deployment performance. The test set must always lie strictly after the training set in chronological order. an R² of 0.85 indicates that the model captures most of the variance in demand but leaves fifteen percent unexplained. RMSE, or root mean squared error, gives the average prediction error per bike and penalizes larger errors more heavily than will small ones; this matters because a prediction error of five hundred bikes during peak hours is substantially more costly operationally than making off by fifty during a quiet night. MAE, the mean absolute error, is a fairer measure that regards all mistakes equitably. Putting all three together paints a fuller picture not only, may two models have similar R² but different error profiles, also the RMSE-to-MAE ratio tells us whether a model's mistakes are spread out evenly or tend toward extremes. Beyond aggregate numbers, the evaluation also includes comparisons along two axes. In addition to the aggregate model comparison, a component-level analysis is planned for the best-performing architecture, in which individual components are removed to quantify their respective contributions to the overall prediction accuracy. The first is individual versus hybrid do hybrid architectures really produce better results than the base lines, and if they do how much? The second axis is cost versus results if a model has twice as many parameters, then it must make realistically better predictions; if not, then the simpler model is preferable. Overfitting is assessed by comparing each model's training set accuracy against its actual test set performance a large gap between the two is an indicator that the model has memorised training patterns without generalising to unseen data. Wherever possible, time of day and weather conditions are also considered in analyses of prediction errors because a model that performs well on aggregate metrics but systematically underperforms during evening rush hours or rainy mornings may be of limited operational value."

P416: [Heading 3] '4.4.3 TCN-LSTM'
P417: [Normal] 'The TCN-LSTM is the first of two hybrid architectures introduced\u2002in this thesis and combines two established "Temporal Convolutional" and "Recurrent" paradigms, as introduced in Sections 2.1.5, 2.1.3 within a shared sequence-to-sequence architecture. P431: [Normal] 'The TCN-GRU-Attention is an extension of the sequential hybrid from Section 4.4.3 where we replace the LSTM by a GRU and add a multi-head attention layer between the recurrent output and\u2002prediction head layers. P492: [Heading 2] '5.1 Evaluation Methodology'
P493: [Normal] 'All models were tested under the strict one-step-ahead temporal protocol specified in Section 3.4. The training set consists of the initial 80 per cent of the time series (December 2017 to mid-September 2018) whereas the test set includes the last 20 per cent (mid-September to November of that year), without temporal overlap between each partition. Each model takes in a 24-hour window as input and outputs one scalar prediction for the hour that follows. Notably, all information about the target hour stays out of input in any pre-processing, feature engineering and training step.'
P494: [Normal] 'Several studies on the Seoul Bike Sharing dataset have been published. This work follows a protocol that is a slight extension of work undertaken by several previous research over the same data. In contrast, the current work guarantees that the test set is future with respect to training data, yielding accuracy figures that genuinely reflect performance in operational deployments.'

MAE treats all errors equally regardless of magnitude. The ratio of RMSE to MAE is a diagnostic for error concentration when the two are close, errors are evenly distributed; when RMSE far exceeds MAE the model makes large outlier error.'
