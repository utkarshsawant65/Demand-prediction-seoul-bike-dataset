Sathishkumar V E & Yongyun Cho (2020), "A rule-based model for Seoul Bike sharing demand prediction using weather data"    

Role: This is your cornerstone paper for establishing the state-of-the-art (SOTA).

Argument: This paper explicitly uses the Seoul Bike dataset and reports the highest performance metric found in the literature for a non-deep-learning model: an R-squared (R²) of 95% on the test set using the CUBIST model. This directly supports your choice of CUBIST as the "best result" to reproduce, fulfilling your professor's primary requirement.   

Aghe, Magnus (2023), "Predicting Rental Demand for Public Bike Sharing - A Case Study of the Seoul Bike Sharing System"    

Role: This paper provides context for the SOTA and reinforces the performance of classical models.

Argument: This study also uses the Seoul dataset and compares a wide range of standard machine learning models, finding that a Stacking Regressor achieved a high R² of 89.56%. This demonstrates that well-tuned machine learning can perform strongly, but also highlights that the CUBIST model's 95% score is an exceptional benchmark.   

Min, Soah & Jung, Yoonsuh (2021), "Comparative study of prediction models for public bicycle demand in Seoul"    

Role: This paper justifies the pivot from classical models to deep learning, directly aligning with your professor's initial project description.

Argument: This study uses daily data from 389 Seoul bike stations and compares traditional models (like Vector Autoregression) with a baseline LSTM model. It argues that LSTMs are more effective for modeling sequential or time-series information, providing the foundational argument for why your thesis must move beyond static models like CUBIST to explore deep learning architectures.   

Phase 2: Justifying the Proposed Innovations
This is where you defend your novel contribution. We have research to support both the primary path (Plan A) and the fallback plan (Plan B).

Plan A: Justification for the GNN-TFT (Spatio-Temporal) Model
This plan has three components to justify: the move to a spatio-temporal perspective (GNNs), the choice of the TFT architecture, and the novel "semantic" component.

Ma, Xinwei, et al. (2022), "Short-Term Prediction of Bike-Sharing Demand Using Multi-Source Data: A Spatial-Temporal Graph Attentional LSTM Approach"    

Role: This paper provides the core argument for why a spatial model is necessary.

Argument: The authors propose a GCN-LSTM model and state that models aggregating demand at the city level are "unsatisfactorily reliable" because they neglect the interactions between stations. This directly supports your decision to pursue a spatio-temporal model using station-level data.   

Rühmann, Sebastian, et al. (2024), "Interpretable Bike-Sharing Activity Prediction with a Temporal Fusion Transformer..."    

Role: This is your "silver bullet" for choosing the Temporal Fusion Transformer (TFT).

Argument: This is the most direct evidence available. The study compares a TFT directly against a state-of-the-art LSTM on a bike-sharing dataset and finds that the TFT model achieves a 36.8% improvement in RMSE. It also highlights the TFT's superior interpretability. This paper provides concrete, quantitative proof that the TFT is the correct architectural choice for the temporal component of your model.   

Li, Xinyu, et al. (2023), "Improving short-term bike sharing demand forecast through an irregular convolutional neural network"    

Role: This paper provides the theoretical and empirical foundation for your core innovation: the "semantic graph."

Argument: This is the key to your GNN's novelty. The authors argue that traditional spatial models based on physical proximity are flawed because "areas that are far apart can be relatively more similar in temporal usage patterns". They propose the concept of "semantic neighbors"—stations connected by functional similarity rather than distance. They prove that a model using a graph built on this concept (using metrics like Pearson Correlation or Dynamic Time Warping) outperforms traditional models. This paper gives you the direct justification for building your GNN on a semantic graph, which is the most innovative part of your proposal.   

Plan B: Justification for the SA-TFT (Advanced Temporal) Model
This is your fallback plan. The innovation here is purely temporal: refining the TFT architecture with an "inductive bias" for seasonality.

Wen, Qingsong, et al. (2022), "A Survey on Transformers in Time Series"

Role: This survey paper establishes the context for your refinement.

Argument: It highlights that effectively modeling seasonality is a key challenge for Transformers in time series forecasting. This validates that your proposed refinement is addressing a known and important problem in the field.

Wen, Qingsong, et al. (2021), "DeepFS: Bridging Self-Attention and Time Series Decomposition for Accurate Forecasting"

Role: This paper provides a concrete example of the exact type of architectural refinement you are proposing.

Argument: The authors propose a model that injects a "periodic inductive bias" into the architecture using Fourier series to explicitly capture seasonality. This is a direct parallel to your proposal of a "Structured-Attention" mechanism that is biased towards daily and weekly patterns. It proves your idea is grounded in established research concepts for improving Transformer performance on time-series data.
